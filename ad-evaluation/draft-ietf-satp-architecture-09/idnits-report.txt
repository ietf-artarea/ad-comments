89	1.  Introduction

91	   This document proposes an interoperability architecture based on
92	   gateways, which are points of interconnection between asset networks
93	   or systems.

95	   There are several services that may be offered by a gateway, one of
96	   which being the direct transfer of a digital asset from one network
97	   to another via pairs of gateways without a mediating third party.

99	   A given asset network or system may have one or more gateways to
100	   perform a unidirectional direct transfer of digital assets to another
101	   network possessing one or more compatible gateway.

103	   The peer gateways must implement a secure asset transfer protocol
104	   that must satisfy certain security, privacy, atomicity and liveliness
105	   requirements.

107	   The purpose of this architecture document is to provide technical
108	   framework within which to define the required properties of a gateway
109	   that supports the secure asset transfer protocol.

111	2.  How to read this document

113	   The current interoperability architecture document provides
114	   background concepts pertaining to the unidirectional transfer of a
115	   digital asset from one asset-network to another.  This includes
116	   concepts around commitment agreement mechanisms or subprotocols, such
117	   as the 2-Phase Commit (2PC), which is at the heart of a
118	   unidirectional transfer.

120	   Readers who are new to asset transfer scenarios in the various
121	   industries (e.g. banking, real-world assets, supply chains, etc.) are
122	   directed to the SAT use-cases document [CASE].

124	   Readers who seek detailed technical information regarding the SAT
125	   asset transfer protocol should read the current document before
126	   proceeding to the SAT-Core document [CORE].

128	3.  Terminology

130	   The current architcture specification borrows existing terminology
131	   from NIST [NISTBC] and ISO [ISOBC].  New terms have been introduced
132	   notably in relation to the use of the gateways paradigm and
133	   commitment subprotocols.

135	   *  Asset-network (system): The network or system where an asset is
136	      digitally represented (e.g., as a token).  The remainder of the
137	      document uses the term "network" to mean asset-systems or asset-
138	      networks (including their ledgers where applicable).

140	   *  Secure Asset Transfer Protocol: The protocol used to transfer
141	      (move) a digital asset from one network to another using gateways.

143	   *  Origin network: The current asset-network where the digital asset
144	      is located.

146	   *  Destination network: The asset-network to which a digital asset is
147	      to be transferred.

149	   *  Two-Party Commitment Agreement: The current interoperability
150	      architecture utilizes the classic two-party commitment agreement
151	      strategy borrowed from distributed database transactions
152	      management [Gray81].  The basic idea is for the two parties (two
153	      gateways) to follow a sequence of predefined steps, where at each
154	      step the parties communicate acknowledgement to proceed to the
155	      next step.  This progressive strategy leads to the final step,
156	      also known as FINAL COMMIT, where both parties agree to a
157	      permanent change of state in their backend systems (e.g.,
158	      databases, blockchains, etc.).  This progressive strategy is often
159	      referred to in technical terms as the 2-Phase commitment (2PC)
160	      agreement subprotocol.

162	   *  Resource Domain: The collection of resources and entities
163	      participating within an asset network.  The domain denotes a
164	      boundary for permissible or authorized actions on resources.

166	   *  Interior Resources: The various interior protocols, data
167	      structures and cryptographic constructs that are a core part of an
168	      asset network or system.

170	   *  Exterior Resources: The various protocols, data structures and
171	      cryptographic constructs that are outside of (external to) the
172	      network or system.

174	   *  Gateway: The collection of services which connects to a minimum of
175	      one network or system, and which implements the secure asset
176	      transfer protocol.

178	   *  Entity public-key pair: This the private-public key pairs of an
179	      entity, where the public-key is available and verifiable outside
180	      the network.  Among others, it may be utilized for interactions
181	      with external entities (e.g. communications) located outside the
182	      network.  The term is used to distinguish this public-key other
183	      key-pairs belonging to the same entity, but which is only
184	      available within the (private) network.

186	   *  Originator: Person or organization in an origin network seeking
187	      the transfer of a digital asset to a beneficiary located in a
188	      remote network.

190	   *  Beneficiary: Person or organization in an destination network
191	      seeking to receive the transfer of a digital asset to from an
192	      originator located in a remote network.

194	   *  Gateway device identity: The identity of the device implementing
195	      the gateway functions.  The term is used in the sense of the DevID
196	      (IEEE 802.1AR) [DevID] or the EK/AIK keys (in TPM1.2 and TPM2.0)
197	      [TPMdevID].

199	   *  Gateway owner: The entity that owns and operates a gateway within
200	      a network.

202	   *  Application Context-ID: The relevant identifier used by
203	      originator's application and the beneficiary's application to
204	      identify the context of the asset transfer at the gateway level.
205	      The context identifier may also be used to bind the application to
206	      selected gateway for the given transfer instance, identified by a
207	      Session-ID.

209	   *  Gateway Session-ID: This is the identifier used between the sender
210	      gateway G1 and the recipient gateway G2 to identify the specific
211	      transfer instance between them.  The Session-ID value MUST be
212	      included in all messages between the peer gateways in a transfer
213	      instance.

215	4.  Assumptions and Principles

217	   The following assumptions and principles underlie the design of the
218	   current gateway architecture, and correspond to the design principles
219	   of the Internet architecture.

221	4.1.  Design Principles

223	   *  Opaque network resources: The interior resources of each network
224	      is assumed to be opaque to (hidden from) external entities.  Any
225	      resources to be made accessible to an external entity must be made
226	      explicitly accessible by a gateway with proper authorization.

228	   *  Externalization of value: The asset transfer protocol is agnostic
229	      (oblivious) to the economic or monetary value (if any) of the
230	      digital asset being transferred.

232	   The opaque resources principle permits the architecture to be applied
233	   in cases where one (or both) networks are private (closed
234	   membership).  It is the analog of the autonomous systems principle in
235	   IP networking [Clar88], where interior routes in local subnets are
236	   not visible to other external networks.

238	   The value-externalization principle permits an asset transfer
239	   protocol to be designed for efficiency, security and reliability --
240	   independent of the changes in the perceived economic value of the
241	   digital asset.  It is the analog of the end-to-end principle in the
242	   Internet architecture [SRC84], where contextual information is placed
243	   at the endpoints of the transfer.

245	4.2.  Operational Assumptions

247	   The following conditions are assumed to have occurred, leading to the
248	   invocation of the asset transfer protocol between two gateways:

250	   *  Application level context establishment: The transfer request from
251	      an Originator utilizing an application (App1) in the origin
252	      network is assumed to have occurred, and that some context-
253	      identifier has subsequently been derived by the respective
254	      applications (App1 and App2).  Furthermore, this context-
255	      identifier is assumed to have been delivered by each application
256	      to its corresponding gateway, permiting each gateway to internally
257	      bind the transfer session-identifier to that context-identifier.

259	   *  Identification of asset to be transferred: The applications at the
260	      originator and the beneficiary are assumed to have identified the
261	      digital asset to be transferred.

263	   *  Identification of originator and beneficiary: The originator and
264	      beneficiary are assumed to have been identified and that consent
265	      has been obtained from both parties regarding the asset transfer.

267	   *  Identification of origin and destination asset networks: The
268	      origin and destination networks is assumed to have been
269	      identified.

271	   *  Selection of gateway: The two corresponding gateways at the origin
272	      and destination networks is assumed to have been identified and
273	      selected.

275	4.3.  Assumptions Regarding Gateway Operators

277	   The following conditions are assumed to have occurred, leading to the
278	   invocation of the asset transfer protocol between two gateways:

280	   *  Identification of gateway-owners: The owners of the two
281	      corresponding gateways are assumed to have been identified and
282	      their ownership status verified.

284	   *  Gateway liabilities: Gateways are performing digital signatures on
285	      messages.  As such, gateway operators are assumed to take on the
286	      relevant liabilities for signing the messages.

288	   *  Gateway message signatures: All messages between gateways are
289	      assumed to be signed and verified (e.g. with X.509).

291	   *  Transitory control of asset by gateway: An asset being transferred
292	      via SAT will technically be controlled by gateway throughout the
293	      transfer duration to ensure the state of the asset is not modified
294	      by another entity.  Gateway owners are liable for the asset
295	      throughout this duration.

297	   *  Network data: Gateways are assumed to have mechanisms in place to
298	      trust data returned from their local networks.  This will depend
299	      on the technical architecture and capabilities of each specific
300	      network.

302	   *  Gateways are trusted: The gateways are assumed to be trusted to
303	      carry-out all the stages of the protocol described in this
304	      architecture [HS19].

306	5.  Gateway Interoperability Modes

308	   The current interoperability architecture based on gateways
309	   recognizes several types of transfer flows:

311	   *  Asset transfer: This refers to the transfer of a digital asset
312	      from the origin network to a destination network, where a
313	      successful asset transfer causes the asset to be extinguished
314	      (burned) in the origin network and be regenerated (minted) at the
315	      destination network [HLP19].

317	   *  Data Sharing: This refers to the transfer of data only under
318	      authorization, in such a way that the data can be verified by a
319	      third party [Abebe19].  The data sharing mode addresses the use-
320	      cases where the state update in one network or system depends on
321	      the existence of state information recorded in a different network
322	      or system [DLVIEW].

324	   *  Asset exchange (swap): This refers to the case where two users are
325	      present in two networks, and they perform concurrent and atomic
326	      swaps of two assets in the two corresponding networks, without
327	      transferring the assets outside (i.e. across) the networks.  The
328	      gateways aid in coordinating the messages pertaining to the swap.

330	   The current SATP architecture can be extended to address the use-
331	   cases pertaining to asset exchanges (swap) between two entities, both
332	   of which are assumed to be present in the origin and destination
333	   asset networks.  Similarly, the SATP architecture can be utilized for
334	   the data transfer mode to report the state of an asset within a
335	   private network, where the gateway acts as an intermediary.  However,
336	   the asset exchange and data transfer mode will be addressed in future
337	   specifications.

339	   The remainder of this architecture document will focus on the asset
340	   transfer flows.

342	6.  Architecture

344	6.1.  Goal of Architecture

346	   The goal of the interoperability architecture is to permit two (2)
347	   gateways belonging to distinct networks to conduct a transfer of
348	   digital assets transfer between them, in a secure, atomic and
349	   verifiable manner.

351	   The asset as understood by the two gateways is expressed in an
352	   standard digital format in a way meaningful to the gateway
353	   syntactically and semantically.

355	   The architecture recognizes that there are different networks
356	   currently in operation and evolving, and that in many cases the
357	   interior technical constructs in these networks maybe incompatible
358	   with one another.

360	   The architecture therefore assumes that in addition to implementing
361	   the bilateral secure asset transfer protocol, a gateway has the role
362	   of making opaque (i.e. hiding) the constructs that are local and
363	   specific to its network.

365	   Overall this approach ensures a high degree of interoperability
366	   across these networks, where each network can operate as a true
367	   autonomous system.  Additionally, this approach permits each network
368	   to evolve its interior technology implementations without affecting
369	   other (external) networks.

371	   The current architecture focuses on unidirectional asset transfers,
372	   although the building blocks in this architecture can be used to
373	   support protocols for bidirectional transfers.

375	   For simplicity the current architecture employs two (2) gateways per
376	   transfer as the basic building block, with one gateway in the origin
377	   and destination networks respectively.  However, the architecture
378	   seeks to be extensible to address future cases involving multiple
379	   gateways at both sides.

381	   The abstract construct of the gateway is used to represent endpoints
382	   that implement the asset transfer protocol interactions and the
383	   business logic that coordinates the transfer protocol steps until
384	   completion satisfying the ACID properties and ensuring liveliness of
385	   the protocol interactions.  In classical distributed databases, this
386	   business logic is often referred to as the transaction manager or the
387	   transaction coordinator.  This architecture specification does not
388	   prescribe any implementations of gateways.

390	6.2.  Overview of Asset Transfer

392	   An asset transfer between two networks is performed using a secure
393	   asset transfer protocol implemented by the gateways in the respective
394	   networks [CORE].  The two gateways implement the protocol in a direct
395	   interaction (unmediated).

397	   A successful transfer results in the asset being extinguished
398	   (burned) at the origin network, and for the asset to be regenerated
399	   (minted) at the destination network.

401	   The secure asset transfer protocol provides a coordination between
402	   the two gateways through the various message flows in the protocol
403	   that is communicated over a secure channel.

405	   The protocol implements a two-party commitment agremeent mechanism
406	   (subprotocol) between the two gateways to ensure that the relevant
407	   properties atomicity, consistency, isolation, and durability (ACID)
408	   are achieved in the transfer.

410	   The mechanism to extinguish (burn) or regenerate (mint) an asset
411	   from/into a network by its gateway is dependent on the specific
412	   network and is outside the scope of the current architecture
413	   specification.  Similarly, the mechanisms used to provide
414	   cryptographic proofs that an asset has been burned or minted in a
415	   given network is also network-specific and therefore out of scope.

417	   As part of the two-party commitment agremeent mechanism, the sender
418	   gateway in the origin network delivers a signed assertion to the
419	   receiver gateway at the destination network which states that asset
420	   in question has been extinguished (burned) from the origin network.

422	   Similarly, the receiver gateway at the destination network in return
423	   delivers a signed assertion to the sender gateway at the origin
424	   network which states that the asset has been regenerated (minted) in
425	   the destination network.

427	   These two tasks are be performed in a synchronized fashion between
428	   the two gateways, following the steps of the two-party commitment
429	   agremeent mechanism.  Adherence to the steps provides sufficient
430	   evidence of the asset transfer that is verifiable by an authorized
431	   third party.

433	   The messages exchanged between the gateways within the two-party
434	   commitment agreement subprotocol are digitally signed by the sender
435	   and transmitted over a secure TLS session.  Each message is
436	   cryptographically bound to the previous message (i.e., includes hash
437	   of previous message) to ensure the integrity of the step-by-step
438	   flows in the commitment agreement subprotocol.  Each message carries
439	   a message-type, Session-ID and timestamp, thereby allowing both
440	   parties to perform the correct sequencing of these messages.

442	   The use of digital signatures on the messages exchanged by the
443	   gateways in commitment agreement subprotocol strengthens the security
444	   properties of the two-party commitment agreement implementation, and
445	   assists in dispute resolutions in the case where one party (or both)
446	   acts in a dishonest way.

448	6.3.  Desirable Properties of Asset Transfer

450	   The desirable features of asset transfers between two gateways
451	   include, but are not limited, to the following:

453	   *  Atomicity: A transfer MUST either commit or entirely fail (failure
454	      means no change to asset state).

456	   *  Consistency: A transfer (commit or fail) MUST always leaves the
457	      networks in a consistent state (i.e. the asset is located in one
458	      network only at any time).

460	   *  Isolation: While the transfer is occurring, the asset state MUST
461	      NOT be modified in the origin network.

463	   *  Durability: Once a transfer has been committed by both gateways,
464	      it MUST remain so regardless of subsequent gateway crashes.

466	   *  Liveliness and safety: The asset transfer protocol results in both
467	      gateways reaching a non-blocking state (commits or aborts)
468	      satisfying the above ACID properties.

470	   *  Verifiable by authorized third parties: The proof that the asset
471	      has been extinguished in the origin network, and the proof that
472	      the asset has been generated in the destination network MUST be
473	      verifiable by an authorized third party.

475	   An implementation of the asset transfer protocol MUST satisfy these
476	   properties, independent of whether the implementation employs
477	   stateful messaging or stateless messaging between the two gateways.

479	   Performing an asset transfer safely and securely is not simply a
480	   matter of communicating desire or intent between two systems
481	   represented by gateways, though such communication is a necessary
482	   part of asset transfer.  The systems, or at least their gateway
483	   proxies, must be interoperable in order to transfer assets among
484	   themselves, but such interoperability imposes strictly more demands
485	   on systems managing digital assets, especially systems that are built
486	   on distributed ledgers, compared to conventional communication
487	   interoperability.

489	   Communication interoperability, which is concerned with syntax and
490	   semantics of information geared towards producing a common
491	   understanding (or knowledge reconciliation) among systems, is
492	   insufficient to fulfill an asset transfer that requires systems to
493	   carry out state updates in concert with each other.  But
494	   communication, or messaging standards, play a necessary and
495	   complementary role to asset transfer protocols.  An exemplar of this
496	   is ISO 20022, which is a comprehensive global standard for financial
497	   messaging that specifies message syntax for common actions occurring
498	   in financial business processes, including payments, credit card
499	   transactions, securities settlements, funds, and trade [ISO20022].
500	   This standard provides the tools to model business processes from
501	   basic logical building blocks and schemas to construct messages using
502	   common formats like XML, JSON, and ASN.1.

504	   As discussed later, such messaging standards are useful to
505	   communicate information about the states of processes and digital
506	   assets across systems, to make requests, and to convey intent.  They
507	   therefore play a necessary and complementary role in asset transfer
508	   protocols.  However they are by themselves insufficient to ensure the
509	   ACID and verifiability properties described earlier.  Another way to
510	   think about the relationship between messaging standards like ISO
511	   20022 and asset transfer protocols is that the former is concerned
512	   with the "what" of cross-system interoperability whereas the latter
513	   is concerned with the "how".  Both kinds of protocols treat systems
514	   as black boxes, but asset transfer protocols must place some
515	   responsibility, and depend, on systems to drive a protocol instance
516	   to successful conclusion.

518	6.4.  Event log-data, crash recovery and backup gateways

520	   Implementations of a gateway MUST maintain event logs and checkpoints
521	   for the purpose of gateway crash recovery.  The log-data generated by
522	   a gateway represents an interior resource that coule be made
523	   accessible to other authorized gateways within the same asset
524	   network.

526	   The mechanism used to provide gateway crash-recovery is dependent on
527	   the specific network.  For interoperability purposes the information
528	   contained in the log and the format of the log-data should be
529	   standardized [CRASH].

531	   The log-data generated by SATP gateways in the context of asset
532	   transfers enables multiple implementations of gateways to serve a
533	   given asset network.  For example, an asset transfer session
534	   interrupted by a crashed gateway could be resumed by another gateway
535	   if the log-data was accessible to the second gateway and was in a
536	   standardized format.

538	   The resumption of an interrupted transfer session (e.g. due to
539	   gateway crash, network failure, etc.) need to take into consideration
540	   the aspects of secure channel establishment and the aspects of the
541	   transfer protocol resumption.  In some cases, a new secure channel
542	   (e.g. TLS session) may need to be established between the two
543	   gateways, before a resumption of the transfer can begin.

545	   The log-data collected by a gateway acts also as a checkpoint
546	   mechanism to assist the recovered (or backup) gateway in continuing
547	   the transfer.  The point at which to re-start the transfer protocol
548	   flow is dependent on the implementation of the gateway recovery
549	   strategy.

551	   The log-data semantics and syntax for SATP gateway crash management
552	   are for future work, and could be developed based on existing log-
553	   data standards such as Syslog [RFC5424] and [RFC3164].

555	6.5.  Overview of the Stages in Asset Transfer

585	   The stages are summarized as follows.

587	   *  Stage 0: Pre-transfer Verification and Context Establishment.  The
588	      two applications utilized by the originator and beneficiary are
589	      assumed to interact as part of the asset transfer.  In this stage,
590	      the applications App1 and App2 may establish some shared transfer
591	      context information (e.g.  Context-ID) at the application level
592	      that will be made available to their respective gateways G1 and
593	      G2.  The verification of the identities of the Originator and
594	      Beneficiary may occur in this stage [FATF].

596	   *  Stage 1: Transfer Initiation and Commencement.  In this stage
597	      gateways G1 and G2 exchange information (claims) regarding the
598	      asset to be transferred, the identity information of the
599	      Originator and Beneficiary and other information regarding
600	      relevant actors (e.g. gateway owners/operators).  The main task in
601	      this stage is for both gateways to finalize the parameters
602	      previously negotiated in Stage 0 and to agree to commence the
603	      transfer.

605	   *  Stage 2: Lock Assertion and Receipt.  In this stage, gateway G1
606	      provides gateway G2 with a signed assertion that the asset in NW1
607	      has been immobilized and under the control on G1.  A signed
608	      assertion is needed because NW1 may be a private or closed
609	      network, and therefore the state-database (ledger) in NW1 is not
610	      readable by external entities including by G2.  This means that
611	      gateway G1 needs to make an explicit signed assertion about the
612	      state in NW1.  Note that the owner/operator of G1 takes on
613	      liability in signing this assertion.

615	   *  Stage 3: Commitment Preparation and Finalization.  In this stage
616	      gateways G1 and G2 commit to the unidirectional asset transfer
617	      using a 2PC (2-phase commitment agreement) subprotocol.

619	   These stages will be further discussed below.

621	7.  Pre-transfer Verification and Context Establishment (Stage-0)

623	   Stage 0 refers to the various verification tasks related to the asset
624	   and the actors involved in a transfer instance, and which need be
625	   carried-out prior to commencing the transfer in Stage 1.

627	   Several tasks need to be conducted as part of the pre-transfer stage:

629	   *  Application level ContextID establishment: The application (App1)
630	      used by the originator and the application (App2) used by the
631	      beneficiary MUST establish a transfer context identifier
632	      (contextID) to uniquely identity the transfer at the application
633	      level.

635	   *  Identification of the asset in the origin network: The specific
636	      asset in the origin network NW1 need to be located and identified,
637	      and its ownership MUST be verified by the sender gateway G1.  A
638	      gateway MUST NOT transfer assets whose ownership is unverified.
639	      Examples of identification syntax for digital assets can be found
640	      in [ISO20022] or ITIN [ITIN].

642	   *  Verification of the class or type of asset: The receiving gateway
643	      G2 MUST verify the class or type of asset that is to be
644	      transferred by gateway G1 in network NW1.  This is to ensure that
645	      the asset type/class conforms to the governing policies in the
646	      destination network NW2.  Additionally, gateway G2 must ensure
647	      that network NW2 can technologically receive (mint) the asset of
648	      that given type/class.  Asset schema definitons, asset profiles
649	      and token metadata may assist in this vertification process.

651	   *  Validation of asset ownership status: The gateway G1 in the origin
652	      network NW1 MUST validate the ownership of the asset to be
653	      transferred prior to beginning the transfer.  This is ensure that
654	      the asset to be transferred to an external network NW2 is owned by
655	      the originator who is requesting the transfer.

657	   *  Authorization to transfer: The gateway G1 MUST obtain
658	      authorization from the owner of the asset (originator) to perform
659	      the transfer to the beneficiary in network NW2.  Similarly, the
660	      gateway G2 serving network NW2 MUST obtain authorization from the
661	      beneficiary to receive the transfer and assign the asset to the
662	      beneficiary in NW2.

664	   *  Exchange of Travel Rule information: The Travel Rule [FATF]
665	      pertains to the information regarding the owner of the asset
666	      (originator) in NW1 and the intended recipient (beneficiary) in
667	      NW2 of the transfer.  In some jurisdictons, the information about
668	      the originator and the beneficiary must be exchanged (transmitted
669	      respectively) prior to the transfer of the asset.

671	   *  Validation of the gateway ownership: The gateways G1 and G2 need a
672	      mechanism to enable them to validate the identity of the owner
673	      (operator) of the gateways respectively, and the fact of the legal
674	      ownership of the gateway.  Examples of ownership verification
675	      mechanism include X.509 certificates, directories of gateways and
676	      owners, and others.

678	   *  Mutual device attestations: In cases where device attestation
679	      [RATS] is required, each gateway need to yield attestation
680	      evidence to the other regarding its configuration.  A gateway may
681	      take on the role as a attestation verifier, or it may rely on an
682	      external verifier to appraise the received evidence [HS19].

684	   *  Negotiation of transfer protocol and network parameters: Gateway
685	      G1 and G2 need to agree on the parameters to be employed within
686	      the transfer instance.  Examples include endpoints definitions for
687	      resources, duration of time-outs of messages, type of commitment
688	      agreement subprotocol (e.g. 2PC), signature algorithms, average
689	      lock-time durations in their respective networks, and others.

691	   The current specification seeks to reuse as much as possible the
692	   existing standards related to digital assets.  We seek to rely on
693	   existing messaging standards like ISO 20022 [ISO20022] or ITIN [ITIN]
694	   for gateway ownership validation, owner status validation, asset
695	   profile identification, and communication of travel rule and transfer
696	   context information.  For identification of digital assets maintained
697	   by distributed ledgers or blockchain systems, we can also rely on
698	   standards like ITIN [ITIN].

700	8.  Transfer Initiation and Commencement (Stage-1)

702	   In Stage 1 the sender gateway (G1) and the receiver gateway (G2)
703	   explicitly accept the parameters of the transfer which were
704	   negotiated in the pre-transfer stage (Stage 0).

706	   This explicit acceptance of the parameters takes the form of gateway
707	   G1 sending a signed Transfer Proposal message containing a Transfer
708	   Initiation Claims set (namely the parameters agreed upon in Stage 0),
709	   and for the gateway G2 to respond with a signed Proposal Receipt
710	   message which carries a hash of the proposal message.

741	   There are several steps that may occur in Stage 1:

743	   *  Secure channel establishment between G1 and G2: This includes the
744	      mutual verification of the gateway device identities and the
745	      exchange of the relevant parameters for secure channel
746	      establishment.

748	   *  Transfer Proposal message (1.1): Gateway G1 sends a signed
749	      transfer proposal message that contains the Transfer Initiation
750	      Claims to gateway G2.  The claims carry the parameters negotiated
751	      in Stage 0 (pre-transfer negotiations).

753	   *  Proposal Receipt (1.2): The gateway G2 indicates acceptance of the
754	      parameters in the Transfer Initiation Claims by way of sending a
755	      signed Proposal Receipt message to G1.  If gateway G2 decides not
756	      to accept parameters in the Transfer Initiation Claims, then G2
757	      can send an abort message to G1, or simply ignore the message
758	      (time-out).

760	   *  Transfer Commence message (1.3): Once gateway G1 receives the
761	      signed Proposal Receipt from gateway G2, gateway G1 is ready to
762	      signal the commencement of the asset transfer.  This is done by
763	      gateway G1 sending a signed Transfer Commence message to G2.

765	   *  Commence Acknowledgement message (1.4): Gateway G2 accepts the
766	      formal commencement of the transfer by responding with a signed
767	      Commence ACK message.

769	   It is important to note the logical separation between the transfer
770	   proposal/receipt messages from the commencement messages.  This
771	   separation allows the gateways to decline to proceed during the
772	   proposal finalization (1.1 and 1.2), prior to starting the commitment
773	   agreement subprotocol (2PC) which formally begins at the Commence
774	   messages (1.3 and 1.4).

776	   This logical separation is useful because in some implementations the
777	   decision to start the commencement (1.3 and 1.4) implies that the
778	   gateways and network have sufficient resource to complete the
779	   transfer.  Gateways that experience extreme loads may use this
780	   separation to slightly delay the commencement until their loads
781	   subsides.

783	   Note that some implementations may choose to enable a multi-round
784	   interactions for steps 1.1 and 1.2.

786	9.  Asset Lock Assertion and Receipt (Stage 2)

788	   In this stage, gateway G1 issues a signed assertion that the asset in
789	   origin network NW1 has been immobilized and under the control of G1.

791	   The steps of Stage 2 are summarized in Figure 4, and broadly consists
792	   of the following:

794	   *  G1 Lock/Escrow Asset (2.1): Gateway G1 proceeds to establish a
795	      lock or escrow the asset belonging to the originator.  This
796	      prevents other local transactions in NW1 from changing the state
797	      of the asset until such time the lock by G1 is finalized or
798	      released.  A time-lock or escrow may also be employed.

800	   *  Lock Assertion (2.2): Gateway G1 sends a digitally signed
801	      assertion regarding the locked (escrowed or immobilized) state on
802	      the asset in network NW1.  The signature by G1 is performed using
803	      its entity public-key pair.  This signature signifies that G1
804	      (i.e. its owner/operator) is standing behind its statement
805	      regarding the locked/escrowed state on the asset.

807	   *  G2 Logs Lock-Assertion Information (2.3): Gateway G2 logs/records
808	      a copy of the signed lock-assertion message received in Step 2.4
809	      to its local state data DB2.  G2 may also notify the fact of the
810	      lock-assertion to all members of network NW2.

812	   *  Lock-Assertion Receipt (2.4): If gateway G2 accepts the signed
813	      assertion from G1, then G2 responds with a digitally signed
814	      receipt message which includes a hash of the previous lock-
815	      assertion message.  The signature by G2 is performed using its
816	      entity public-key pair.  Otherwise, if G2 declines accepting the
817	      assertion then G2 can simply ignore the transfer and let the
818	      session time-out (i.e. transfer attempt has failed).

843	   NW2 are discovered later.

847	   proof) in the case of later denial by G2.

849	10.  Commitment Preparation and Finalization (Stage 3)

851	   In Stage 3 the gateways G1 and G2 commit to the asset transfer by
852	   making permanent the changes they made to the respective asset
853	   networks.  The previous signed receipt message (2.4) from gateway G2
854	   to gateway G1 signals the start of the commitment subprotocol in
855	   Stage 3.

857	   Upon receiving the signed receipt message from G2 in the previous
858	   stage, G1 begins the commitment (see Figure 5):

860	   *  Commit-prepare (3.1): Gateway G1 indicates to G2 to prepare for
861	      the commitment of the transfer.  This message MUST include hashes
862	      of the previous messages (message 2.2 and 2.4).

864	   *  Temporary asset mint (3.2): Gateway G2 creates (mints) an
865	      equivalent asset in NW2 assigned to itself as the owner.  This
866	      step can be reversed (i.e. asset destroyed) in the case of the
867	      failure in the commitment steps because G2 is still the owner of
868	      the asset in NW2.

870	   *  Commit-ready (3.3): Gateway G2 sends a commit-ready message to G1
871	      indicating that it is ready to carry-out the last steps of the
872	      commitment agreement subprotocol.  Note that that the entire asset
873	      transfer session can be aborted before this step without affecting
874	      the asset state in the respective networks.

876	   *  Asset burn (3.4): Gateway G1 extinguishes (burns) the asset in
877	      network NW1 which it has locked since Step 2.3.

879	   *  Commit-final assertion (3.5): Gateway G1 indicates to G2 that G1
880	      has performed the extinguishment of the asset in NW1.

882	   *  Asset-assignment (3.6): Gateway G2 assigns the minted asset (which
883	      it has been self-holding since Step 3.2) to the Beneficiary.

885	   *  ACK-final receipt (3.7): Gateway G2 sends a signed assertion that
886	      it has assigned the asset to the intended Beneficiary.

888	   *  Record receipt (3.8): Gateway G1 logs/records a copy of the signed
889	      receipt message to its local state data DB1.  G1 may also notify
890	      the fact of the signed receipt to all members of network NW1.

892	   *  Transfer complete (3.9): Gateway G1 terminates the asset transfer
893	      session with gateway G2.  This allows both sides to close down the
894	      secure TLS channel established earlier in Stage 1.

928	11.  The Commitment Agreement Sub-protocol

930	   Within Stage 3, the gateways implement a transactional commitment
931	   agreement sub-protocols that permit the coordination between two
932	   gateways, and the final commitment of the unidirectional transfer of
933	   the asset.

935	   In the case that there are multiple commitment subprotocols supported
936	   by the gateways, the choice of the sub-protocol (type/version) and
937	   the corresponding commitment evidence must be negotiated between the
938	   gateways during Stage 0.

940	   For example, in Stage 2 and Stage 3 discussed above the gateways G1
941	   and G2 may implement the classic commitment aggrement subprotocol
942	   [Gray81] as a means to ensure efficient and non-disputable
943	   commitments to the asset transfer.

945	   Historically, transactional commitment agreement protocols in
946	   distributed databases employ locking mechanisms to prevent update
947	   conflicts on the data item in question.  When used within the context
948	   of digital asset transfers across networks, the fact that an asset
949	   has been locked in NW1 needs to be communicated via an assertion to
950	   G2 in an indisputable manner.

952	   Similarly, G2 here returns a signed assertion to G1 that the asset
953	   has been regenerated (minted) in NW2.

955	   These signed assertions MUST be verifiable by an authorized third
956	   party, in the case that disputes occur (post event) or where audit is
957	   required on the asset transfer.

959	   The assertions (claims) signed by the gateways MUST use a standard
960	   format, such as the Secure Assertions Markup Language (SAML)
961	   [SAML2.0] or JSON Web-Tokens (JWT) [RFC7519].  The current
962	   interoperability architecture consider assertions formats as out of
963	   scope, and seeks to make use of these existing standards.  The choice
964	   of the format for signed assertions must be agreed upon by peer
965	   gateways G1 and G2 within Stage-0.

967	12.  Security Considerations

969	   As an asset network holds an increasing number of digital assets, it
970	   may become attractive to attackers seeking to compromise the
971	   cryptographic keys of the entities, services and its end-users.

973	   Gateways are of particular interest to attackers because they enable
974	   the transferal of digital assets to external networks, which may or
975	   may not be regulated.  As such, hardening technologies and tamper-
976	   resistant crypto-processors (e.g. TPM, SGX) should be used for
977	   implementations of gateways [HS19].

979	   The SAT protocol faces challenges with regards to the confidentiality
980	   of a transfer between gateways, and the potential issue related to a
981	   denial-of-service (and resource waste) when either gateway is not
982	   compliant with the protocol.

984	   For confidentiality of a transfer, the secure asset transfer protocol
985	   MUST utilize a TLS1.3 secure channel established between the sender
986	   gateway (G1) and the receiver gateway (G2).  The two gateways
987	   establish this secure channel at the start of Stage 1 before they can
988	   proceed to execute the asset transfer protocol.  This includes both
989	   gateways verifying all the relevant parameters required for their TLS
990	   session (e.g. correct TLS endpoints, certificate validation, identity
991	   validation, etc.).

993	   There are several challenges that may arise when gateways are not
994	   compliant with the SAT protocol.  Some of these are described below.

996	12.1.  Multiple intentional aborts by the sender gateway

998	   A dishonest sender gateway G1 may purposely fail to continue the
999	   protocol run at certain crucial points.  One such crucial point is in
1000	   Stage-3, where the gateway G1 is expected to transmit the Commit-
1001	   Final Assertion message (3.5).  If the gateway G1 intentionally fails
1002	   to transmit this message, gateway G2 may conclude that the message
1003	   has been lost and may proceed to reverse the temporary hold it has
1004	   previously created (temporary asset mint in message 3.2).  Although
1005	   this dishonest behavior by G1 does not cause asset damage to G2 or
1006	   NW2, it may exhaust computing resources at gateway G2.  If network
1007	   NW2 incurs transaction fees, such a reversal may be costly for
1008	   gateway G2.

1010	12.2.  Multiple intentional aborts by the receiver gateway

1012	   In a similar manner, a receiver gateway G2 may also purposely fail to
1013	   continue the protocol run at certain crucial points.  One such point
1014	   is the Commit-Ready message (3.3) that it should transmit to G1 after
1015	   receiving the commit prepare message (3.1) from G1.  In this case,
1016	   gateway G1 may conclude that the message is lost and simply abort the
1017	   protocol run.

1019	   Another possible denial-of-service attack could arise when G2
1020	   purposefully fails to send a Lock-Assertion-Receipt (2.4), thereby
1021	   forcing G1 to reverse its lock that was performed earlier (2.1).

1023	12.3.  Failure to transmit ACK-Final Receipt

1025	   Another possible point of attack by a dishonest gateway G2 may occur
1026	   by the gateway intentionally failing to transmit the ACK-Final-
1027	   Receipt (3.7) in response to the Commit-Final Assertion message (3.5)
1028	   from gateway G1.  Here, the sender gateway G1 may conclude that the
1029	   message is lost and will assume that the transaction has reached
1030	   completion in network NW2.  The sender gateway G1 has retained the
1031	   previous Lock-Assertion Receipt (2.4) in Stage-2 that was signed by
1032	   G2, indicating that the gateway G2 has accepted the responsibility of
1033	   ensuring that the asset-assignment (3.6) by G2 will be correctly
1034	   executed.  Failure by G2 to complete this task may become a liability
1035	   for the owner of gateway G2.

1037	   In general, it is recommended that multiple redundant gateways be
1038	   utilized within a network to mitigate a single gateway's malicious
1039	   behavior.  Furthermore, there are gateway recovery and failover
1040	   mechanisms that have been defined in [CRASH].

1042	12.4.  Failure to extinguish asset

1044	   Another potential attack may come from a dishonest gateway G1 who
1045	   intentionally fails to extinguish the asset in network NW1 (in step
1046	   3.4).  This means G1 is henceforth in control of the asset belonging
1047	   to the originator.

1049	   This This type of denial-of-service could be considered a network-
1050	   specific limitation because it implies that G1 was able to perform a
1051	   lock on the asset in network NW1 on behalf of an asset-owner without
1052	   accountability.  Several asset networks currently support solutions
1053	   to this problem by way of introducing a temporary third-party trusted
1054	   holder (custodian) in the network for the duration of a transaction.

1056	   This denial-of-service is out of scope for the current architecture
1057	   specification because it represents a weakness on the part of the
1058	   network NW1.

1060	12.5.  Identity impersonations

1062	   Another vector of attack may involve a gateway that impersonates an
1063	   asset holder in a given network.  For example, a gateway G1 may
1064	   pretend to be the owner of an asset (originator) in network NW1 and
1065	   proceed to transfer it to a beneficiary located in network NW2.

1067	   The verification of the identity of the originator and beneficiary
1068	   MUST be performed as part of the set-up stage (Stage 0) as described
1069	   above.

1071	   The identity verification includes that of the owner of gateways G1
1072	   and G2 respectively.  Standard protocols for federated identity
1073	   management already exist and have wide deployment.

1075	13.  Policy Considerations

1077	   Digital asset transfers are policy-driven in the sense that it must
1078	   observe and enforce the policies defined each of the respective
1079	   networks.  Resources that make-up a network are owned and operated by
1080	   entities (e.g. typically persons or organizations), and these
1081	   entities typically operate within regulatory jurisdictions.  It is
1082	   the responsibility of these entities to translate regulatory policies
1083	   into functions on networks that comply to the relevant regulatory
1084	   policies.

1086	   At the application layer, asset transfers must take into
1087	   consideration the status of assets and incorporate relevant asset-
1088	   related policies into their business logic.  These policies must
1089	   permeate down to the gateways that implement the functions of asset
1090	   transaction processing.

1092	14.  Compatibility Considerations

1094	   The current architecture for the secure transfer of assets between
1095	   two networks is designed to be agnostic to the identification and the
1096	   format of the asset within the origin and destination networks.  This
1097	   enables the architecture to be compatible with a broad range of
1098	   assets identification schemes (e.g. ITIN [ITIN]), asset description
1099	   syntaxes and business processes such as ISO 20022.  A key part of
1100	   Stage 0 is to enable the gateways in the respective networks to
1101	   facilitate the exchange information about the asset state, its
1102	   identification model, and formats.  This facilitation enables
1103	   gateways and networks to determine whether they can process incoming
1104	   transfers prior to commencing Stage 1.
