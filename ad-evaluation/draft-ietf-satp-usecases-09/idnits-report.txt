94	1.  Introduction

96	   Business networks, built on both centralized and decentralized
97	   models, have emerged to manage cross-organization assets and
98	   workflows.  The scope of such workflows and the assets they govern,
99	   as well as the set of participating organizations within a network,
100	   have been quite limited, partly for security, privacy, and
101	   scalability reasons, and partly because organizations have been
102	   reticent to moving large portions of their pre existing workflows to
103	   such networks.  This is especially observable in the areas of trade,
104	   finance, supply chain logistics, and property management.  Yet the
105	   workflows managed by these networks are naturally interlinked in the
106	   real world, and therefore cannot afford to remain isolated from each
107	   other technologically, which would diminish the value of their
108	   assets.  At the same time, a network, once built, has institutional
109	   staying power, and it is therefore impractical to assume that they
110	   will expand or merge.  Interoperability is therefore an imperative in
111	   this fragmented business network ecosystem.  This comes in different
112	   flavors, namely the ability to move an asset from one network to
113	   another, interlinking workflows to share asset state with proof of
114	   authenticity from one network to another, and swapping assets in
115	   different networks as part of a business transaction, as listed in
116	   the SAT Architecture Specification [SATA].  The purpose of this
117	   document is to describe prominent examples of these modes that have
118	   been encountered by enterprises and business consortiums and
119	   identified as challenges to be overcome.  In particular, this
120	   document describes scenarios where the Secure Asset Transfer Protocol
121	   (SATP) [SATP] can be directly applied to solve the problem of moving
122	   digital assets across networks, for which no other canonical protocol
123	   exists in the literature.

125	2.  Terminology

127	   The following are some terminology used in the current document, with
128	   some borrowed from [NIST] and [ISO], and new terms introduced only
129	   when needed:

131	   *  Asset network (system): The network or system where a digital
132	      asset is utilized.

134	   *  Secure Asset Transfer Protocol (SATP): The protocol used to
135	      transfer (move) a digital asset from one network to another using
136	      gateways.

138	   *  Origin network: The current network where the digital asset is
139	      located.

141	   *  Destination network: The network to which a digital asset is to be
142	      transferred.

144	   *  Data sharing: The process, using the Asset Transfer Protocol, by
145	      which one or more units of verifiably authentic data are
146	      communicated from an Origin network to a Destination network,
147	      either voluntarily or upon request.

149	   *  Asset Transfer: A fail-safe process of moving an asset from one
150	      network to another, with the destruction of the asset in the
151	      Origin network and its recreation in the Destination network
152	      occurring as a single atomic action.

154	   *  Asset Exchange: A fail-safe process of exchanging (or swapping)
155	      assets held by a pair of owners, each asset being maintained in a
156	      different network, with the two in-network transfers occurring as
157	      a single atomic action.

159	3.  International Trade and Supply Chains

161	3.1.  Trade Finance and Logistics

163	   There are several real-world examples of consortium networks managing
164	   different aspects of international trade.  Networks like We.Trade
165	   [WET], built on Hyperledger Fabric [HLF], and Marco Polo [MP], built
166	   on R3 Corda [R3C], manage trade finance workflows by connecting
167	   exporters, importers, and financial institutions (primarily banks).
168	   Other networks like TradeLens [TL], built on Hyperledger Fabric,
169	   manage trade shipping and documentation logistics, by connecting
170	   exporters and shipping carriers.  As an example, consider a system of

256	   the larger export-import workflow.  But these documents are also
257	   digital assets in their own rights.

260	   shipped, and hence can be traded as a security or used as collateral
261	   against debt obligations in the financial market.  Hence, Step 11 in

278	3.2.  Tracking Food Shipments

280	   The use case linking a trade finance network with a trade logistics
281	   network can be augmented by adding a food tracking network like the
282	   IBM Food Trust [IFT] to the mix.  Such a network connects producers,
283	   suppliers, manufactures, and retailers, who participate in food
284	   supply chains.  Purchase orders, like those negotiated between
285	   producers and retailers, and which are illustrated as negotiated
286	   between exporter and importers in Figure 2, are recorded in this
287	   network’s ledger.  For quality control, its business workflow will
288	   track at periodic intervals the state (e.g., temperature and
289	   humidity) of containers carrying, for example, produce from farm to
290	   source port and from destination port to warehouse.  The trade
291	   logistics network handles documentation and dispatch but does not
292	   track the location or condition of a consignment outside of a
293	   carrier’s purview.  Clearly, these networks play complementary roles
294	   in a supply chain.  The logistics network should be able to get the
295	   state and history of a container before dispatch from the food
296	   tracking network, as should the latter from the former after the
297	   carrier has delivered a consignment.  End-to-end supply chain
298	   visibility and effectiveness relies on the interoperability of these
299	   two networks, or to be precise, their ability to share verifiably
300	   authentic data with each other.  Further, such interoperation also
301	   enables the trade finance network to allow the creation of a letter
302	   of credit only after verifying the existence of a valid purchase
303	   order in the food tracking network.  Figure 3 illustrates the links
304	   between these networks.

369	4.  Currency and Finance

371	   The emerging paradigm of Decentralized Finance (DeFi) and the
372	   emerging application of Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) have
373	   opened up a spectrum of scenarios that require management of
374	   financial digital assets across multiple systems, typically built on
375	   distributed ledgers.

377	   DeFi is a “new financial paradigm that leverages distributed ledger
378	   technologies to offer services such as lending, investing, or
379	   exchanging cryptoassets without relying on a traditional centralized
380	   intermediary” [BISDeFi].  Following the Web3 philosophy [Web3],
381	   scoped for the world of finance, DeFi offers architecture and
382	   protocols built on smart contracts deployed on blockchain or other
383	   distributed ledger technology.  It thereby obviates the need for
384	   centralized management and orchestration of financial processes
385	   (e.g., currency transfers, exchanges, securities settlements) by
386	   trusted authorities who can gain undue leverage.

388	   CBDC is a form of tokenized cryptocurrency that various central banks
389	   around the world are experimenting with as the digital equivalent of
390	   traditional central bank-issued money used by banks and other
391	   financial institutions as well as end users for commercial
392	   transactions and settlements.  Central banks possess exclusive
393	   authority to mint and issue money in physical cash form and in the
394	   form of electronic reserves.  They also support commercial bank money
395	   used in retail transactions by banks and other users in their private
396	   capacities.  Central banks have traditionally used their control over
397	   these different forms of money to enforce monetary policy in a way
398	   that promotes financial stability and provides broad access to safe
399	   and efficient payments [BISCBDC].  CBDCs would form a new, or
400	   alternative, type of central bank money, typically (but not always)
401	   built on blockchain or other distributed ledger technology.  They
402	   have recently garnered significant interest in government circles by
403	   promising increased access and inclusion, better resilience, and
404	   increased scale and efficiency of currency transfers, compared to
405	   traditional forms of central bank-issued or central bank-backed
406	   currency.

408	   CBDCs can broadly be classified into “wholesale” and “retail”.
409	   Wholesale CBDC, which facilitates inter-bank and cross-border
410	   settlements, is currency that is available only to banks and other
411	   financial institutions.  Retail CBDC is available to the public and
412	   can be used as a digital form of cash, enabling fast transparent
413	   payments for goods and services at high scale and volume; in effect,
414	   it can be used as a substitute for legacy payment mechanisms.

416	   Different system architectures exist to manage CBDC for banks and end
417	   users, from issuance to transfers to redemptions.  A 2-tier model as
418	   illustrated in Figure 5 has recently gained popularity, where
419	   wholesale CBDC networks manage interactions between central and
420	   commercial banks, and retail CBDC networks manage interactions
421	   between commercial banks and end users.  If the role of the central
422	   bank is treated as the defining characteristic of a system
423	   architecture, this model can be referred to as “indirect”, because
424	   commercial banks mediate claims between the central bank and end
425	   users and also facilitate payments.  Other architectures also exist,
426	   including “direct CBDC”, where the central bank issues CBDC directly
427	   to end users and facilitates payments, and “hybrid CBDC”, which
428	   provides users the facility to make direct claims on the central bank
429	   while allowing intermediaries to facilitate payments [BISRCBDC].

431	4.1.  Currency Transfers

433	   The 2-tier “indirect CBDC” model illustrated in Figure 5 presents
434	   unique interoperability challenges that require protocols for asset
435	   transfers and which SATP is well-suited to handle.  In the higher
436	   tier lie wholesale CBDC networks, bringing together central or
437	   reserve banks and various commercial banks.  Following the DeFi
438	   logic, these networks are typically built on distributed ledger and
439	   smart contract technologies.  Commercial banks hold reserve currency
440	   deposits with the reserve bank, which has the special power to mint
441	   currency and issue CBDC and also enforce regulatory compliance.  In
442	   the lower tier lie retail CBDC networks for commercial banks and
443	   their customers, built on similar technologies, enabling seamless,
444	   efficient, and transparent payments using CBDCs.  A retail CBDC
445	   network may involve a single commercial bank or multiple commercial
446	   banks, depending on the market caps of those banks and their purposes
447	   for joining such a network.

497	   approve (or at least audit) the transfer of currency from one retail

499	   world of decentralized finance, or DeFi for short, currency cannot
500	   afford to remain siloed in any single CBDC network.  Hence, these
501	   networks must be interoperable in order to facilitate secure
502	   transfers of currency among themselves, as illustrated in Figure 5.

504	   Two specific instances of currency transfer across networks can be
505	   observed in this example: one from a wholesale CBDC network to a
506	   retail CBDC network, and another from one retail CBDC network to
507	   another.  Since currency in tokenized form is a digital asset, these
508	   scenarios require the direct application of a secure protocol for
509	   asset transfer.  SATP [SATP] fits the bill, is agnostic of the types
510	   of distributed ledger technologies on which the respective networks
511	   are built, and simply requires the networks to use SATP gateways.
512	   This is not just a theoretical proposition; a candidate design for a
513	   bridge between Hyperledger Fabric [HLF] and Hyperledger Besu [HLB]
514	   networks using SATP and the Hyperledger Cacti interoperability
515	   platform [HLC] has been proposed by distributed ledger researchers
516	   [Aug23].

518	4.2.  Multi-CBDC Economy

520	   Several governments, banks and financial communities have explored
521	   the use of a shared ledger containing multiple CBDCs as way to
522	   potentially obtain an economy of scale in the development and
523	   maintenance of their own respective CBDCs.  Such a Multi-CBDC
524	   approach has the potential benefit to improve cross-border payments
525	   and protect monetary sovereignty, without necessarily becoming a
526	   monetary union [BISMCBDC].

528	   However, even within a Multi-CBDC configuration, there must be a
529	   mechanism to interconnect each respective national (sovereign) bank
530	   network with the shared Multi-CBDC network.  Gateways appear to be an
531	   attractive means to permit the transfer of a CBDC from one national
532	   network/ledger into the shared Multi-CBDC ledger, and vice versa.
533	   One major requirement is the assurance that consistency is maintained
534	   between the CBDC counts on the national network with that on the
535	   Multi-CBDC network (i.e. no counterfeiting; no double-spend).

537	   With or without a Multi-CBDC ledger, the existence of different
538	   national networks managing different wholesale CBDC assets will
539	   necessitate inter-network transfers for cross-border payments and
540	   settlements [BISCBP] [WBGCBP] [PUbin].  Whether directly between two
541	   wholesale CBDC networks or between a wholesale CBDC network and the
542	   Multi-CBDC network, transfer of currency assets is a problem for
543	   which SATP appears to be the most suitable solution.

545	4.3.  Delivery vs Payment (DvP) of Securities

547	   In Decentralized Finance, or DeFi for short, investors and financial
548	   institutions will form networks to manage the creation and purchase
549	   of securities.  As a simple example, consider a network to which the
550	   Treasury, which issues bonds, and commercial banks, which purchase
551	   and trade bonds, belong.  Also consider a payment network of the kind
552	   seen in Section 3.3 (or a retail CBDC network of the kind seen in
553	   Section 4.1), which allows CBDC transfers between commercial banks’
554	   accounts.  In the securities network, banks may wish to transfer
555	   bonds to each other but only in exchange for compensation.  But such
556	   compensation can be made only on a payment network where the two
557	   maintain currency accounts (e.g., in CBDC).  Therefore, the
558	   securities and payment networks must be able to interoperate in such
559	   a way that two banks can carry out a delivery-vs-payment transaction
560	   spanning these two independent networks.  Such a transaction must be
561	   atomic, i.e., either both bond and CBDC tokens get transferred in
562	   their respective networks or neither gets transferred.  Figure 6
563	   illustrates this exchange.

610	4.4.  Stock Options Contract Fulfilment

612	   Stock options are financial instruments that allow holders to buy or
613	   sell stock at predetermined prices until specific expiration dates
614	   [StOpt].  Exercising an option, i.e., buying or selling a stock by
615	   paying a “strike price”, involves a more complex cross-network
616	   financial transaction than the securities DvP use case.  Option
617	   contracts can be managed and enforced in a specific business network
618	   (which can be labeled a “stock network”) to which entities like an
619	   Options Clearing Corporation (OCC), stock exchanges (e.g., the
620	   Chicago Board Options Exchange), and brokerage firms, belong.  The
621	   OCC is the issuer and guarantor for all options and futures contracts
622	   traded on U.S. exchanges [OCC].  It provides secured back-end
623	   clearing services, while exchanges provide front-end trading markets.
624	   Transfer and settlement of payments, just like in the DvP scenario,
625	   will typically occur in a dedicated payment network in which buyers
626	   and sellers of stock have currency accounts.  Options contract
627	   fulfilment requires the stock and payment networks to work in
628	   concert, and hence need SATP to coordinate transactions across them.

630	   Figure 7 illustrates a simplified flow for the exercising of a “call”
631	   option, where a buyer acquires stock from a seller by paying it a
632	   strike price.  First, the buyer informs its brokerage, which then
633	   notifies its exchange about the buyer's intent to exercise the
634	   option.  The exchange in turn notifies the OCC in the Stock Network.
635	   (All these notifications typically occur via a smart contract).
636	   Steps 1-2 illustrate these notifications.  The OCC then verifies the
637	   contract’s validity and the buyer’s right to exercise the option.  If
638	   this is successful, the OCC triggers a payment request to the buyer
639	   through a contract-to-contract communication from the Stock Network
640	   to the Payment Network (step 3).  (Alternatively, the buyer may
641	   trigger a payment request in the Payment Network, which then requests
642	   the Stock Network for proof of the contract’s validity from the OCC
643	   before approving the payment.)  The buyer then pays the strike price
644	   to the seller (step 4).  The payment receipt is now sent to the Stock
645	   Network for the OCC’s verification (or alternatively, the buyer can
646	   request the OCC to fetch and verify the receipt from the Payment
647	   Network) and fulfilment of the contract (step 5).  The OCC, via the
648	   exchange, requests the brokerage to transfer stock from the seller’s
649	   to the buyer’s account (steps 6-7).

651	   This cross-network transactions require SATP using gateways, either
652	   to communicate information of a contract’s validity (step 3) or a
653	   payment receipt (step 5) with authenticity proof (i.e., data
654	   sharing), or by ensuring that strike price payment (step 4) and
655	   contract fulfilment (step 6-7) occur atomically as an exchange using
656	   a coordinated set of commitments.

697	5.  Decentralized Commerce: Payments for Goods and Services

699	   In the emerging Web3 world, undergirded by blockchain and DLT, goods
700	   and services are increasingly available on decentralized networks,
701	   either as purely digital objects or proxies of physical objects.  The
702	   networks typically manage these assets in the form of non-fungible
703	   tokens (NFTs) using smart contracts and decentralized applications
704	   (DApps for short).  DeFi in a Web3 ecosystem can create thriving
705	   online marketplaces where goods and services can be bought and sold
706	   across network boundaries if those networks are interoperable (i.e.,
707	   assets and data can be moved or managed seamlessly across networks).
708	   SATP is a necessary requirement to enable and secure such a
709	   decentralized form of commerce.

711	5.1.  Transferal of Digital Art and Payments across National Borders

713	   There is currently growing interest within many artist communities of
714	   developing and selling digital-only artwork, in which the artwork
715	   consists of a file in a well-known (e.g., JPEG, MPEG) format that is
716	   created by an artist.  The artists seek to sell copies of the
717	   digital-only artwork on the global marketplace, allowing anyone in
718	   the world to purchase a copy and consume (e.g., display offline) the
719	   artwork at the buyer’s discretion.  Currently, the most popular
720	   technological vehicle to achieve this goal is through the
721	   tokenization of the copies of the artwork coupled with digital
722	   encryption/signature technologies to transfer control (and thereby
723	   legal ownership) of the digital-only artwork to the buyer.

725	   Although there are a number of technical and legal challenges (e.g.,
726	   copyright enforcement) to completing such a sale, one key issue
727	   pertains to the sale and payment for digital-only artwork across
728	   national borders.  Many nations enforce taxation upon the sale of any
729	   asset, including that of artwork generally both domestically and
730	   internationally.  Thus, when the control/ownership of a tokenized
731	   digital-only artwork is transferred to a new owner in a foreign
732	   nation and payment is received, taxation must be obtained at the
733	   point-of-sale (which could be an online platform) and proof of
734	   delivery must be traceable to ensure that no taxation-avoidance
735	   occurs.  A secure asset transfer protocol between systems that can be
736	   built on distributed or shared ledgers via gateways with designated
737	   legal authority is necessary to enforce governmental regulations and
738	   provide accountability.

740	5.2.  Payment for Streaming Services

742	   Streaming services like Netflix and IPTV can tap into the
743	   cryptocurrency and DeFi user markets by offering content on
744	   distributed ledger networks using smart contracts.  Instead of
745	   monthly subscriptions, they can offer pay-as-you-go streaming of
746	   arbitrarily low amounts to large numbers of users on such networks.
747	   Content transfer can be backed by NFTs, which users can redeem on any
748	   video or audio playback application.  Because here already exist
749	   several payment networks for unregulated (e.g., Bitcoin) and
750	   regulated (e.g., CBDC) currencies, the content networks are likely to
751	   remain independent and not involve any monetary transactions on their
752	   own ledgers, as that would create safety and regulatory issues.
753	   Content transfer in a content network can be backed by a
754	   corresponding payment in a payment network if there is a facility to
755	   transfer payment receipts from the latter to the former.  This can be
756	   fulfilled through the data sharing mode of SATP, whereby knowledge of
757	   payment along with authenticity proof can be communicated across
758	   networks.  Alternatively, an atomic swap of the content and the
759	   payment can occur across both networks using the asset exchange mode
760	   of SATP.

762	   In a related example, if users wish to obtain content that is not
763	   available currently with the streaming service provider, that
764	   provider may seek and purchase that content from another distributer
765	   that is offering it on a different content network.  The copyright to
766	   that content can be backed by NFTs and the secure transfer of those
767	   NFTs from one network to another will require SATP.

769	   Though these examples show what is possible, ongoing initiatives in
770	   Europe and China are aimed at facilitating data and asset transfers.
771	   Since 2022, China has issued a series of policies and guidelines to
772	   promote the development of a nationwide data market that supports
773	   data elements' assetization, capitalization, and circulation
774	   [CNMeas].  A multi-layered national data infrastructure is slated be
775	   built by the end of 2029 to support transaction, transmission, and
776	   utilization, of data; telecom operators like China Mobile and China
777	   Unicom are building large- scale networks to enable data transmission
778	   [CNDatInf].  Design and implementation of this infrastructure will be
779	   guided by the newly established National Data Standardization
780	   Technical Committee (TC609), which will coordinate with existing data
781	   circulation standards organizations like ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 32 and ISO/
782	   IEC JTC 1/SC 42, and reuse existing standards.

784	   In Europe, several projects like Gaia-X [GaiaX] and Catena-X
785	   [CatenaX] aim to create secure, decentralized, and interoperable data
786	   infrastructures.  Catena-X specifically targets data exchanges in
787	   automotive industry operations while Gaia-X is a more generic, EU-
788	   wide data infrastructure.  The International Data Spaces Association
789	   (IDSA) produces several ISO standards like ISO/IEC AWI 20151 and ISO/
790	   IEC 23751:2022 for exchanges across data spaces [IDSA].  The IDSA
791	   also partners with the Eclipse Foundation to implement open-source
792	   solutions [EclDS] and jointly develop the Dataspace Protocol (DSP),
793	   using which data usage policies, usage contracts, and schemas can be
794	   negotiated (in the so-called "control plane") between two data-
795	   exchange gateway peers [DSP].  The actual post-negotiation data
796	   transfer (in the so-called "data plane") is out of scope of DSP, and
797	   here SATP can fill the gap.

799	6.  Trustworthy DNS Resource Record Migrations using an Augmented EPP

801	   Several examples have been presented in this document where SATP is
802	   indispensable for enabling cross-network asset and data transfer and
803	   transaction settlement.  But SATP can also be used to augment an
804	   existing protocol by making it more secure, trustworthy, and
805	   efficient rather than creating an altogether new feature that
806	   preexisting technology was unable to provide.  Conventional Internet
807	   architecture offers scope for such augmentation.  Though highly
808	   decentralized, the Internet consists of nodes (or actors) that play
809	   critical roles in various protocols.  Such protocols would benefit if
810	   the actions of these actors can be recorded and tracked on blockchain
811	   or other decentralized ledger networks.  (It must be noted that the
812	   performance implications of these augmentations ought to be studied
813	   before firm recommendations are made.)

815	   In the canonical DNS architecture [RFC1034] [RFC1035], multiple
816	   registries and registrars may co-exist, managing DNS records for
817	   domain administrators (registrants).  The Extensible Provisioning
818	   Protocol (EPP) allows registrars to communicate with registries for
819	   DNS resource record updates [RFC5730].  Consider an example where R
820	   acts as a TLD (top-level domain) registry for the “.example”
821	   namespace, and entities S1 and S2 are registrars that can issue SLDs
822	   (second-level domains) to users.  In one scenario, user Alice, who
823	   administers an SLD (say alice.example) issued by S1, wishes to
824	   transfer that SLD to S2.  She can submit a request to S2 for this
825	   purpose, following which the respective registrars (S1 and S2) can
826	   communicate with the registry (R) using EPP to transfer the SLD from
827	   S1 to S2.  S2 will then reissue the domain to Alice.  In another
828	   scenario, Alice can transfer administration of her S1-issued domain
829	   to user Bob, a registrant who relies on S2 for registrations.

831	   Though EPP facilitates these resource updates and transfers, and
832	   reputed registrars offer users these features in a usable manner,
833	   such scenarios face safety issues as they require trust and proper
834	   coordination among the participants.  These issues can be mitigated
835	   by distributed ledger networks, smart contracts, and NFTs, which
836	   collectively provide a platform for safe and transparent DNS resource
837	   transfers with easy detection of non-compliance.

839	   In such a scheme, domains (or domain names) can be monetized as NFTs
840	   or digital assets, e.g., in the form of ERC-721 [ERC721] tokens, a
841	   popular NFT standard in the Ethereum ecosystem [Ethereum].  The
842	   creation of a domain is represented by minting of an NFT and the
843	   deletion of a domain by the burning of the NFT using smart contracts
844	   whose transactions are tracked on a distributed ledger maintained by
845	   a network of entities.  Registrants Alice and Bob also participate in
846	   the smart contracts and hold wallet identities, allowing them to
847	   trade assets by submitting transactions to the ledger.  Registrants,
848	   registrars, and registries can engage in commercial transactions
849	   using contracts governing tokenized (and monetized) domains for the
850	   purposes of issuing and transferring domains (among users and
851	   registrars).  This setup provides more safety when registrars are not
852	   reputed, and also allows new registrar services to emerge and build
853	   reputations.  It also enables users like Alice and Bob to transfer
854	   domains to each other safely and seamlessly.

856	   SATP is crucial to enabling this scheme where registrars belong to,
857	   and issue, NFT-backed domains in different ledgers maintained by
858	   different networks.  Ensuring that tokens are securely and atomically
859	   transferred between the ledgers will be a necessary prerequisite for
860	   an EPP resource record update operation.  Further, non-compliance, or
861	   non-fulfilment of EPP, by either registrar, can be easily detected by
862	   examining the SATP logs from the respective networks’ gateways,
863	   thereby safeguarding the process.  In future, reputed registrars may
864	   require their counterparties to comply with SATP-based transfers
865	   across ledgers as a prerequisite to engaging in a domain transfer
866	   with them.

868	   This scenario additionally illustrates an example of what typically
869	   occurs in Stage 0 of a SATP instance [SATP], which is where
870	   negotiation and agreement-building occurs between gateways.  EPP
871	   instances among registrars and registries will occur in this stage,
872	   both prior to the start of an SATP instance and after its conclusion.
873	   EPP will aid in the generation of transaction contexts, which
874	   gateways need to transfer an asset (in this case, a tokenized domain)
875	   using SATP.  Many other applications of SATP for asset transfers may
876	   similarly augment or bolster pre-existing business workflows, which
877	   (like EPP here) will produce transactions contexts for SATP
878	   instances.

880	7.  Interoperation Protocol Considerations

882	   The use cases provided as examples serve to illustrate instances of
883	   general phenomena that the Secure Asset Transfer Protocol [SATP],
884	   with a limited number of variations, is designed to handle.  The data
885	   sharing examples in Section 3 can be extrapolated to any kinds of
886	   data that need to be shared between networks running arbitrary
887	   workflows.  The asset transfer example in Section 4.1 and the asset
888	   exchange example in Section 4.3 similarly can be extrapolated to any
889	   kinds of digital assets lying within any kind of network.
890	   Considerations for the interoperability protocol, or SATP, can
891	   therefore be limited to standard distributed systems issues like
892	   integrity, fault tolerance, and liveness, while completely
893	   disregarding the nature of the assets, networks, and workflows, which
894	   can all remain opaque to the protocol.
