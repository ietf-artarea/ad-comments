17	Abstract

19	   This memo describes the Secure Asset Transfer Protocol (SATP) for
20	   digital assets.  SATP is a protocol operating between two gateways
21	   that conducts the transfer of a digital asset from one gateway to
22	   another, each representing their corresponding digital asset
23	   networks.  The protocol establishes a secure channel between the
24	   endpoints and implements a 2-phase commit (2PC) to ensure the
25	   properties of transfer atomicity, consistency, isolation and
26	   durability.

167	1.  Introduction

169	   This memo proposes a secure asset transfer protocol (SATP) that is
170	   intended to be deployed between two gateway endpoints to transfer a
171	   digital asset from an origin asset network to a destination asset
172	   network.  Readers are directed first to [ARCH] for a description of
173	   the architecture underlying the current protocol.

175	   Both the origin and destination asset networks are assumed to be
176	   opaque in the sense that the interior construct of a given network is
177	   not read/write accessible to unauthorized entities.

179	   The protocol utilizes the asset burn-and-mint paradigm whereby the
180	   asset to be transferred is permanently disabled or destroyed (burned)
181	   at the origin asset network and is re-generated (minted) at the
182	   destination asset network.  This is achieved through the coordinated
183	   actions of the peer gateways handling the unidirectional transfer at
184	   the respective networks.

186	   A gateway is assumed to be trusted to perform the tasks involved in
187	   the asset transfer.

189	   The overall aim of the protocol is to ensure that the state of assets
190	   in the origin and destination networks remain consistent, and that
191	   asset movements into (out of) networks via gateways can be accounted
192	   for.

194	   There are several desirable technical properties of the protocol.
195	   The protocol must ensure that the properties of atomicity,
196	   consistency, isolation, and durability (ACID) are satisfied.

198	   The requirement of consistency implies that the asset transfer
199	   protocol always leaves both asset networks in a consistent state
200	   (that the asset is located in one system/network only at any time).

202	   Atomicity means that the protocol must guarantee that either the
203	   transfer commits (completes) or entirely fails, where failure is
204	   taken to mean there is no change to the state of the asset in the
205	   origin (sender) asset network.

207	   The property of isolation means that while a transfer is occurring to
208	   a digital asset from an origin network, no other state changes can
209	   occur to the asset.

211	   The property of durability means that once the transfer has been
212	   committed by both gateways, that this commitment must hold regardless
213	   of subsequent unavailability (e.g. crash) of the gateways
214	   implementing the SATP protocol.

216	   All messages exchanged between gateways are assumed to run over
217	   TLS1.3.  HTTPS/S must be used instead of plain HTTP.

219	   The endpoints at the respective gateways should provide access to
220	   credentials (or other identification mechanisms) to prove the legal
221	   owner (or operator) of the gateway.  An example of credentials
222	   include X509 certificates.

241	      distributed ledger technology or similar technology [MICA].

243	   *  Asset network: A monolithic system or a set of distributed systems
244	      that manage digital assets.

246	   *  Client application: This is the application employed by a user to
247	      interact with a gateway.

249	   *  Gateway: The computer system functionally capable of acting as a
250	      gateway in an asset transfer.

264	   *  Gateway Claim: An assertion made by a Gateway regarding the status

269	   In the remainder of this document, for brevity of description the

272	4.  The Secure Asset Transfer Protocol

274	4.1.  Overview

276	   The Secure Asset Transfer Protocol (SATP) is a gateway-to-gateway
277	   protocol used by a sender gateway with a recipient gateway to perform
278	   a unidirectional transfer of a digital asset.

280	   The protocol defines a number of API endpoints, resources and
281	   identifier definitions, and message flows corresponding to the asset
282	   transfer between the two gateways.

284	   The current document pertains to the interaction between gateways
285	   through API2 [ARCH].

288	                    |  Client  |                | Off-net  |
289	             ------ |   (App)  |                | Resource |

305	4.2.  SATP Model

307	   The model for SATP is shown in Figure 1 [ARCH].  The Client
308	   (application) interacts with its local gateway (G1) over an interface
309	   (API1) in order to provide instructions to the gateway with regards
310	   to actions to assets and related resources located in the local
311	   system or network (NW1).

313	   Gateways interact with each other over a gateway interface (API2).  A
314	   given gateway may be required to access resources that are not
315	   located in network NW1 or network NW2.  Access to these types of
316	   resources are performed over an off-network interface (API3).

318	4.3.  Stages of the Protocol

320	   The SATP protocol defines three (3) stages for a unidirectional asset
321	   transfer:

323	   *  Transfer Initiation stage (Stage-1): These flows deal with
324	      commencing a transfer from one gateway to another.  In this stage
325	      the sender gateway delivers a proposal containing the parameters
326	      agreed upon in Stage-0.

328	   *  Lock-Assertion stage (Stage-2): These flows deal with the
329	      conveyance of signed assertions from the sender gateway to the
330	      receiver gateway regarding the locked status of an asset at the
331	      origin network.

333	   *  Commitment Preparation and Finalization stage (Stage-3): These
334	      flows deal with the asset transfer and commitment establishment
335	      between two gateways.

337	   In order to clarify discussion, the interactions between the peer
338	   gateways prior to the transfer initiation stage is referred to as the
339	   setup stage (Stage-0), which is outside the scope of the current
340	   specification.

342	   The Stage-1, Stage-2 and Stage-3 flows will be discussed below.

344	4.4.  Gateway Cryptographic Keys

346	   SATP recognizes the following cryptographic keys which are intended
347	   for distinct purposes within the different stages of the protocol.

349	   *  Gateway signature public key-pair: This is the key-pair utilized
350	      by a gateway to digitally sign assertions and receipts.

352	   *  Gateway secure channel establishment public key-pair: This is the
353	      key-pair utilized by peer gateways to establish a secure channel
354	      (e.g. TLS1.3) for a transfer session.

356	   *  Gateway identity public key pair: This is the key-pair that
357	      uniquely identifies a gateway.

359	   *  Gateway-owner identity public key pair: This is the key-pair that
360	      identifies the owner (e.g. legal entity) who is the legal owner of
361	      a gateway.

363	   When peer gateways deliver public-keys, these are expressed in JSON
364	   Web Key (JWK) format [RFC7517].

366	   This document assumes that the relevant X.509 certificates are
367	   associated with these keys.  However, the mechanisms to obtain X.509
368	   certificates is outside the scope of this specification.

370	5.  SATP Message Format, identifiers and Descriptors

372	5.1.  Overview

374	   This section describes the SATP message-types, the format of the
375	   messages exchanged between two gateways, the format for resource
376	   descriptors and other related parameters.

378	   The mandatory fields are determined by the message type exchanged
379	   between the two gateways (see Section 7).

381	5.2.  SATP Message Digital Signatures and Key Types

383	   All SATP messages exchanged between gateways must be signed [ECDSA],
384	   using the JSON Web Signatures mechanism [RFC7515].

386	   Signature algorithms used by gateways for SATP messages SHOULD be
387	   selected from those defined in the JSON Web Algorithms (JWA)
388	   specification [RFC7518], with key types defined in JSON Web Key (JWK)
389	   specification [RFC7517].

391	   The choice of signature algorithm and key-type must be agreed upon
392	   between the gateways prior to the commencement of the SATP protocol
393	   session.  The agreed values are then included within the Transfer
394	   Initialization Claim body in Transfer Proposal Message.

396	   All SATP implementations MUST implement at minimal the ECDSA
397	   signature algorithm with the P-256 curve and the SHA-256 hash
398	   function.

400	   Additional signature algorithms and keying parameters may be
401	   negotiated by peer gateways.  However, the negotiation protocol is
402	   outside the scope of this specification.

404	5.3.  SATP Message Format and Payloads

406	   SATP messages are exchanged between peer gateways, where depending on
407	   the message type one gateway may act as a client of the other (and
408	   vice versa).

410	   All SATP messages exchanged between gateways are in JSON format
411	   [RFC8259].

413	5.3.1.  Protocol version

415	   This refers to SATP protocol Version, encoded as "major.minor"
416	   (separated by a period symbol).

418	   The current version is "1.0" defined in this specification.
419	   Implementations not understanding a future option value should return
420	   an appropriate error response and cease the negotiation.

422	5.3.2.  Message Type

424	   This refers to the type of request or response to be conveyed in the
425	   message.

427	   The possible values are defined in the IANA SATP Message Types
428	   Registry Section 13.3, Paragraph 1:

430	   *  transfer-proposal-msg: This is the transfer proposal message from
431	      the sender gateway carrying the set of proposed parameters for the
432	      transfer.

434	   *  proposal-receipt-msg: This is the signed receipt message
435	      indicating acceptance of the proposal by the receiver gateway.

437	   *  reject-msg: This is a reject message from a gateway to the peer
438	      gateway in the session, indication the reason and the resulting
439	      action.

441	   *  transfer-commence-msg: Request to begin the commencement of the
442	      asset transfer.

444	   *  ack-commence-msg: Response to accept the commencement of the asset
445	      transfer.

447	   *  lock-assert-msg: Sender gateway has performed the lock of the
448	      asset in the origin network.

450	   *  assertion-receipt-msg: Receiver gateway acknowledges receiving the
451	      signed lock-assert-msg.

453	   *  commit-prepare-msg: Sender gateway requests the start of the
454	      commitment stage.

456	   *  ack-prepare-msg: Receiver gateway acknowledges receiving the
457	      previous commit-prepare-msg and agrees to start the commitment
458	      stage.

460	   *  commit-final-msg: Sender gateway has performed the extinguishment
461	      (burn) of the asset in the origin network.

463	   *  ack-commit-final-msg: Receiver gateway acknowledges receiving the
464	      signed commit-final-msg and has performed the asset creation and
465	      assignment in the destination network.

467	   *  commit-transfer-complete-msg: Sender gateway indicates closure of
468	      the current transfer session.

470	   *  error-msg: This message is used to indicate that an error has
471	      occurred at the SATP layer.  It can be transmitted by either
472	      gateways.

474	   *  session-abort-msg: This message is used by a gateway to abort the
475	      current session.

477	5.3.3.  Digital Asset Identifier

479	   This is the identifier that uniquely identifies the digital asset in
480	   the origin network which is to be transferred to the destination
481	   network.

483	   The digital asset identifier is a value that is derived by the
484	   applications utilized by the originator and the beneficiary prior to
485	   starting the asset transfer.

487	   The mechanism used to derive the digital asset identifier is outside
488	   the scope of the current document.

490	   The digital asset identifier is a JSON object, which may be encoded
491	   as a string in base64 [RFC4648].

493	5.3.4.  Asset Profile Identifier

495	   This is the unique identifier of the asset schema or asset profile
496	   which defines the class or type of asset in question.  The asset
497	   profile is relevant from a regulatory perspective.

499	   In some cases the profile identifier may be needed by the receiver
500	   gateway at the destination network in order to evaluate whether the
501	   asset is permitted to enter the destination network.

503	   The default format of the asset profile identifier is JSON, with
504	   base64 encoding.

506	   The formal specification of asset profiles and their identification
507	   is outside the scope of this document.

509	5.3.5.  Gateway Network ID (NetworkID)

511	   The network identifier (NetworkID) is the unique alphanumeric string
512	   representing the asset network behind a gateway.  A gateway may
513	   simultaneously stand in front of multiple asset networks.  As such,
514	   for a specific asset transfer instance both the sender gateway and
515	   recipient gateway must indicate which asset networks are the origin
516	   network and destination network respectively.

518	   The network identifier values of the origin network
519	   (senderGatewayNetworkId) and destination network
520	   (recipientGatewayNetworkId) must be communicated and agreed upon
521	   prior to the commencement of the asset transfer.  This selection is
522	   confirmed by peer gateways in the Transfer Initialization Claim that
523	   is transmitted within Transfer Proposal Message.

525	   The mechanism to allocate globally unique network identifier is
526	   outside the scope of the current specification.

528	5.3.6.  Transfer-Context ID:

530	   This is the unique immutable identifier representing the application
531	   layer context of a single unidirectional transfer.  The method to
532	   generate the transfer-context ID is outside the scope of the current
533	   document.

535	   The transfer-context may be a complex data structure that contains
536	   all information related to a SATP execution instance.  Examples of
537	   information contained in a transfer-context may include identifiers
538	   of sessions, gateways, networks or assets related to the specific
539	   SATP execution instance.  The sender gateway provides this value to
540	   the receiver gateway.

542	   The default format of the transfer context identifier is JSON, with
543	   base64 encoding.

545	   The Transfer Context ID (transferContextId) value is established by
546	   the sender application (possibly with the assistance of the sender
547	   gateway) in the origin network.  The value is then communicated to
548	   the receiving application in the destination network prior to the
549	   commencement of the SATP protocol.  Both the sender gateway and
550	   receiver gateway must understand how to process the transferContextId
551	   value.  The value is used in the Transfer Proposal Message (with
552	   message type satp:msgtype:transfer-proposal-msg) between the two
553	   gateways.

555	   The mechanism to derive the Transfer Context ID value and to
556	   communicate it between the applications is outside the scope of the
557	   current specification.

559	5.3.7.  Session ID:

561	   This is the unique identifier representing a session between two
562	   gateways handling a single unidirectional transfer.  This may be
563	   derived from the Transfer-Context ID at the application level.  There
564	   may be several session IDs related to a SATP execution instance.
565	   Only one Session ID may be active for a given SATP execution
566	   instance.  Session IDs may be stored in the transfer-context for
567	   audit trail purposes.

569	   The sender gateway provides this value to the receiver gateway.

571	5.3.8.  Client Credential Types Supported by Gateways

573	   SATP Gateways must support JSON Web Tokens (JWT) [RFC7519] with
574	   OAUth2.0 [RFC6749] as the minimal credential type for authenticating
575	   incoming API calls from Client Applications (see Figure 1).

577	   A gateway may support additional credential mechanisms, which may be
578	   advertised by the gateway through different mechanisms (e.g. config
579	   file at a well-known endpoint).  However, these mechanisms are out of
580	   scope for the current specification.

582	5.3.9.  Gateway Supported TLS Schemes

584	   Gateways must support TLS1.3 [RFC8446].

586	   The TLS scheme is used by peer gateways to establish the TLS session
587	   prior to the commencement of an asset transfer.  Gateways must a
588	   minimal support the AES-128 in GCM mode with SHA-256
589	   (TLS_AES_128_GCM_SHA256).

591	   If the client (sender gateway) transmits a list of supported
592	   credential schemes, the server (recipient gateway) selects one
593	   acceptable credential scheme from the offered schemes.

595	   If no acceptable credential scheme was offered, a "unsupported
596	   gatewayTlsScheme" (err_1.1.34) reject message is returned by the
597	   server Section 8.5, Paragraph 1.

599	5.3.10.  Client Offers Other Supported TLS Schemes

601	   If a client (sender gateway) wishes to use TLS schemes other than the
602	   basic scheme (AES-128 in GCM mode with SHA-256), then the client may
603	   may choose to send a JSON object listing the client's supported TLS
604	   schemes.

606	   These must be selected from those defined in TLS1.3 [RFC8446].

608	5.3.11.  Gateway Identifier

610	   This is the unique identifier of the gateway service.  The gateway
611	   identifier MUST be uniquely bound to its SATP endpoint (e.g. via
612	   X.509 certificates).

614	   This gateway identifier is distinct from the gateway operator
615	   business identifier (e.g., legal entity identifier (LEI) number).  A
616	   gateway operator may operate multiple gateways.  Each of the gateways
617	   within an asset network MUST be identified by a unique gateway
618	   identifier.

620	   The mechanisms to establish the gateway identifier or the operator
621	   identifier is outside the scope of this specification.

623	5.3.12.  Payload Hash

625	   This is the hash of the current message payload.  This is utilized in
626	   some crucial messages within the SATP message flows to detect errors
627	   or attacks, where the payload-hash of the previously received message
628	   (from a sender gateway) is included in the response message to that
629	   sender gateway.

631	   For example, the hash of the Transfer Proposal message from the
632	   sender gateway is included in the Transfer Commence message.
633	   Similarly, the hash of the lock-assertion message (from the gateway
634	   at the origin network) is included in the Lock Assertion Receipt
635	   Message (sent in response by the gateway at the destination network).

637	5.3.13.  Hash Algorithm Supported

639	   All cryptographic hash operations in the current specification follow
640	   that used for JSON structures, which includes the canonicalization
641	   (normalization) and serialization of the data, prior to the
642	   application of the hash algorithm.

644	   The default hash algorithm that all SATP implementations MUST support
645	   is the SHA-256 algorithm [RFC7515].

647	5.3.14.  Signature Algorithms Supported

649	   This is a JSON list of digital signature algorithms supported by a
650	   gateway.  Each entry in the list should be either an Algorithm Name
651	   value registered in the IANA "JSON Web Signature and Encryption
652	   Algorithms" registry established by [RFC7518] or be a value that
653	   contains a Collision-Resistant Name.

655	   See Section (Section 5.2, Paragraph 1).

657	5.3.15.  Asset Lock Mechanism within a Network

659	   SATP gateways may be providing service to multiple types of asset
660	   networks, each of which may utilize different local mechanisms to
661	   immobile (lock) a given asset as way to provide exclusion in the case
662	   of multiple attempts to change the state of the asset.

664	   The origin network and the destination network may in fact utilize
665	   distinct asset locking mechanisms, and the type of mechanisms to
666	   immobile (lock) a given asset may have different convergence
667	   (finalization) speeds.  Peer gateways must exchange information about
668	   the asset locking information in their respective network to enable
669	   both gateways to compute an approximate time of convergence
670	   (assetLockExpirationTime) and set timers for the transfer of asset.
671	   A timer that expires too soon may result in the SATP protocol
672	   terminating too early before reaching the final commitment stage.

674	   Currently, the most common type of mechanisms (NetworkLockType) to
675	   temporarily lock an asset in a network are (i) TIME_LOCK, (ii)
676	   HASH_LOCK, (iii) HASH_TIME_LOCK.

678	   Some examples of the use of lock mechanisms are follows.  Bitcoin
679	   [BTC] utilizes a time-lock for delays in some of its operations
680	   (e.g., nLockTime).  Ethereum [ETH] uses a Hash-Lock mechanism for
681	   atomic swaps.  Ethereum and Ripple [XRP] uses Hashed Time-lock in
682	   their contracts (HTLC).

684	   The exact definition of these asset locking mechanisms are network-
685	   dependent, and such are out of the scope of the current work.

687	5.3.16.  Lock assertion Claim Format

689	   This is the format of the claim regarding the state of the asset in
690	   the origin network.  The default format is JSON, with parts being
691	   base64 encoded as needed.

693	   If the sender gateway offers multiple choices of other formats to the
694	   receiver gateway, the selection must occur prior to the establishment
695	   of the session.

697	5.3.17.  Lock assertion Claim

699	   The actual encoded JSON string representation of the claim using the
700	   format as specified by the corresponding Lock Assertion Claim Format
701	   value.

703	5.4.  Negotiation of Security Protocols and Parameters

705	   The peer gateways in SATP must establish a TLS session between them
706	   prior to starting the transfer initiation stage (Stage-0).  The TLS
707	   session continues until the transfer is completed at the end of the
708	   commitment establishment stage (Stage-3).

710	   In the following steps, the sender gateway is referred to as the
711	   client while the receiver gateway as the server.

713	5.4.1.  TLS Secure Channel Establishment

715	   TLS 1.3 MUST be implemented to protect gateway communications.

717	5.4.2.  Client asserts or proves identity

719	   The details of the assertion/verification step are specific to the
720	   chosen credential scheme and are outside the scope of this document.

722	5.4.3.  Messages can now be exchanged

724	   Handshaking is complete at this point, and the client and server can
725	   begin exchanging SATP messages.

727	6.  Overview of Message Flows

729	   The SATP message flows are logically divided into three (3) stages
730	   [ARCH], with the preparatory stage denoted as Stage-0.  How the tasks
731	   are achieved in Stage-0 is out of the scope of this specification.

733	   The Stage-1 flows pertains to the initialization of the transfer
734	   between the two gateways.

736	   After both gateways agree to commence the transfer at the start of
737	   Stage-2, the sender gateway G1 must deliver a signed assertion that
738	   it has correctly performed the relevant action on the asset within
739	   the origin network (NW1).  Examples of actions by G1 include
740	   performing a temporary lock on the asset, or performing a permanent
741	   disablement (burn) of the asset in NW1.

743	   If that signed assertion is accepted by gateway G2, it must in return
744	   transmit a signed receipt to gateway G1 that it has correctly
745	   performed the relevant corresponding action on destination network
746	   (NW2).  Examples of actions by G2 include creating (minting) a
747	   temporary asset under its control in NW2.

749	   The Stage-3 flows commit gateways G1 and G2 to the burn and mint in
750	   Stage-2.  The sender gateway G1 must make the lock on the asset in
751	   the origin network NW1 to be permanent (burn).  The receiver gateway
752	   G2 must assign (mint) the asset in the destination network NW2 to the
753	   correct beneficiary.

755	   The reader is directed to [ARCH] for further discussion of this
756	   model.

805	7.  Identity and Asset Verification Stage (Stage 0)

807	   Prior to commencing the asset transfer from the sender gateway
808	   (client) to the recipient gateway (server), both gateways must
809	   perform a number of verification steps.  The types of information
810	   required by both the sender and recipient are use-case dependent and
811	   asset-type dependent.

813	   The verifications include, but not limited to, the following:

815	   *  Verification of the gateway signature public key: The sender
816	      gateway and receiver gateway must validate their respective
817	      signature public keys that will later be used to sign assertions
818	      and claims.  This may include validating the X509 certificates of
819	      these keys.

821	   *  Gateway owner verification: This is the verification of the
822	      identity (e.g. LEI) of the owners of the gateways.

824	   *  Gateway device and state validation: This is the device
825	      attestation evidence [RFC9334] that a gateway must collect and
826	      convey to each other, where a verifier is assumed to be available
827	      to decode, parse and appraise the evidence.

829	   *  Originator and beneficiary identity verification: This is the
830	      identity and public-key of the entity (originator) in the origin
831	      network seeking to transfer the asset to another entity
832	      (beneficiary) in the destination network.

834	   These are considered out of scope in the current specification, and
835	   are assumed to have been successfully completed prior to the
836	   commencement of the transfer initiation flow.  The reader is directed
837	   to [ARCH] for further discussion regarding Stage-0.

839	8.  Transfer Initiation Stage (Stage 1)

841	   This section describes the transfer initiation stage, where the
842	   sender gateway and the receiver gateway prepare for the start of the
843	   asset transfer.

845	   The sender gateway proposes the set of transfer parameters and asset-
846	   related artifacts for the transfer to the receiver gateway.  These
847	   are contained in the Transfer Initiation Claim.

849	   If the receiver gateway accepts the proposal, it returns a signed
850	   receipt message for the proposal indicating it agrees to proceed to
851	   the next stage.  If the receiver gateway rejects any parameters or
852	   artifacts in the proposal, it can provide a counteroffer to the
853	   sender gateway by responding with a proposal reject message carrying
854	   alternative parameters.

856	   Gateways MUST support the use of the HTTP GET and POST methods for
857	   the endpoint [RFC9110].

859	   Clients (sender gateway) MAY use the HTTP GET or POST methods to send
860	   messages in this stage to the server (recipient gateway).  If using
861	   the HTTP GET method, the request parameters may be serialized using
862	   URI Query String Serialization.

864	8.1.  Transfer Initialization Claim

866	   This is set of artifacts pertaining to the asset that must be agreed
867	   upon between the client (sender gateway) and the server (recipient
868	   gateway).

870	   The format of the identity fields in this message, unless otherwise
871	   stated, is a JSON text.

873	   The Transfer Initialization Claim consists of the following:

875	   *  digitalAssetId REQUIRED: This is the globally unique identifier
876	      for the digital asset located in the origin network.

878	   *  assetProfileId REQUIRED: This is the globally unique identifier
879	      for the asset-profile definition (document) on which the digital
880	      asset was issued.

882	   *  networkLockType REQUIRED: The default locking mechanism used for
883	      an asset.  These can be (i) TIME_LOCK, (ii) HASH_LOCK, (iii)
884	      HASH_TIME_LOCK.

886	   *  assetLockExpirationTime OPTIONAL: The duration of time (in
887	      seconds) for an asset lock to expire in the network, if it is a
888	      HASH_TIME_LOCK or a TIME_LOCK.

890	   *  verifiedOriginatorEntityId REQUIRED: This is the identity data of
891	      the originator entity (person or organization) in the origin
892	      network.  This information must be verified by the sender gateway.

894	   *  verifiedBeneficiaryEntityId REQUIRED: This is the identity data of
895	      the beneficiary entity (person or organization) in the destination
896	      network.  This information must be verified by the receiver
897	      gateway.

900	      owner (originator) in the origin network or system.

902	   *  beneficiaryPublicKey REQUIRED: This is the public key of the
903	      beneficiary in the destination network.

905	   *  senderGatewaySignaturePublicKey REQUIRED: This is the public key
906	      of the key-pair used by the sender gateway to sign assertions and
907	      receipts.

909	   *  receiverGatewaySignaturePublicKey REQUIRED: This is the public key
910	      of the key-pair used by the recevier gateway to sign assertions
911	      and receipts.

913	   *  senderGatewayId REQUIRED: This is the identifier of the sender
914	      gateway.

916	   *  recipientGatewayId REQUIRED: This is the identifier of the
917	      receiver gateway.

1235	9.  Lock Assertion Stage (Stage 2)

1237	   The messages in this stage pertain to the sender gateway providing
1238	   the recipient gateway with a signed assertion that the asset in the
1239	   origin network has been locked or disabled and under the control of
1240	   the sender gateway.

1343	10.  Commitment Preparation and Finalization (Stage 3)

1345	   This section describes the transfer commitment agreement between the
1346	   client (sender gateway) and the server (receiver gateway).

1348	   This stage must be completed within the time specified in the
1349	   lockAssertionExpiration value in the lock-assertion message.  This
1350	   value is the time when the lock or escrow upon the asset will expire
1351	   on the origin network.

1353	   The completion of this stage is denoted by the signed Commit-Final
1354	   Acknowledgement Receipt Message sent from the receiver gateway
1355	   (server) to the sender gateway (client).  If the
1356	   lockAssertionExpiration timer at the client expires before the
1357	   Commit-Final Acknowledgement Receipt Message is received by the
1358	   client, the client may terminate the session.

1360	   The flow follows a request-response model.  The client makes a
1361	   request (POST) to the Transfer Commitment endpoint at the server.

1363	   Gateways MUST support the use of the HTTP GET and POST methods for
1364	   the endpoint.

1366	   Clients MAY use the HTTP GET or POST methods to send messages in this
1367	   stage to the server.  If using the HTTP GET method, the request
1368	   parameters may be serialized using URI Query String Serialization.

1370	   The client and server may be required to sign certain messages in
1371	   order to provide standalone proof (for non-repudiation) independent
1372	   of the secure channel between the client and server.  This proof may
1373	   be required for audit verifications post-event.

1375	   (NOTE: Flows occur over TLS.  Nonces are not shown).

1636	   discussion.

1638	11.  Error Messages

1640	   SATP distinguishes between session termination initiated by the user
1641	   at the application layer from session termination cased by errors at
1642	   the SATP protocol layer.

1644	   A gateway can transmit an error message at any point in the SATP
1645	   protocol flow to its peer gateway.

1647	   The default action to be taken by the transitting gateway is to
1648	   terminate the session immediately.

1650	   Error messages at the SATP protocol layer is distinct from time-outs
1651	   due to gateway crashes.

1653	11.1.  Session Termination Notification

1655	   Session closure initiated at the application layer is not considered
1656	   to be an error at the SATP protocol layer.

1658	   The message type used for application-initiated session termination:
1659	   session-abort-msg.

1661	   The message type used to indicate protocols errors: error-msg.

1663	   A gateway can transmit the session abort message at any point in the
1664	   SATP protocol flow.  No further messages will be sent by the gateway.

1666	   Any data received after the session termination message MUST be
1667	   ignored.

1669	11.2.  Connection Errors

1671	   Errors may occur at the connection layer, independent of the flows at
1672	   the SATP layer and errors there.

1674	   (a) connectionError: There is an error in the TLS session
1675	   establishment (TLS error codes should be reported-up to the gateway
1676	   level)

1678	   (b) badCertificate: The gateway TLS certificate was corrupt,
1679	   contained signatures, that did not verify correctly, etc.  (Some
1680	   common TLS level errors: unsupported_certificate,
1681	   certificate_revoked, certificate_expired, certificate_unknown,
1682	   unknown_ca).

1684	   Connection errors resulting in the time-out of the session MUST
1685	   result in the termination of the transfer session.  In the case of a
1686	   transfer session termination, gateways SHOULD release its local
1687	   computing resources and release asset-locks in their respective
1688	   networks.

1690	11.3.  SATP Protocol Errors

1692	   The errors at the SATP level pertain to protocol flow and the
1693	   information carried within each message.  These are enumerated in the
1694	   appendix.

1696	11.4.  Effectiveness of Session Aborts

1698	   The effectiveness of a session-abort message on the state of the
1699	   asset depends on where the abort message occurs in the SATP protocol
1700	   flow in Figure 2.

1702	   Note that a session-abort message maybe lost and never be received by
1703	   the peer gateway.  Gateways can crash prior to receiving an abort
1704	   message.

1706	   If gateway G2 transmits a session-abort message after gateway G1
1707	   performs a lock (msgtype:lock-assert-msg) on the asset in network
1708	   NW1, the gateway G1 can always unlock the asset and restore its
1709	   state.

1711	   If either gateway G1 or gateway G2 transmits a session-abort message
1712	   after gateway G1 sends a lock-assert message (msgtype:lock-assert-
1713	   msg) but before G2 sends the commit ready message (msgtype:commit-
1714	   ready-msg), the gateway G1 can always unlock the asset and restore
1715	   its state in network NW1.

1717	   Similarly, if either gateway G1 or gateway G2 transmits a session-
1718	   abort message immediately after gateway G1 sends a commit-prepare
1719	   message (msgtype:commit-prepare-msg) but before G2 sends the commit
1720	   ready message (msgtype:commit-ready-msg), the gateway G2 can always
1721	   reverse the changes made by G2 to NW2 (i.e. reverse the assignment-
1722	   to-self of the minted asset).

1724	   However, an abort message (occurring in either direction) after
1725	   gateway G1 transmits the commit final message (msgtype:commit-final-
1726	   msg) will not be effective.  This is because G1 has already burned
1727	   the asset in NW1 and G2 has already minted the asset in NW2 and has
1728	   legally agreed to assign the asset to the appropriate beneficiary in
1729	   NW2.

1731	   In general, the termination of sessions or aborts occurring before
1732	   the sender gateway G1 disables (burns) the asset in NW1 (in flow 3.4
1733	   in Figure 2) will incur a minimal cost in terms of computing
1734	   resources or fees on the part of both gateways G1 and G2.

1736	12.  Security Consideration

1738	   Gateways may be of interest to attackers because they enable the
1739	   transferal of digital assets across networks and therefore are an
1740	   important function in the digital economy.

1742	   *  Disruptions in transfers and denial of service: Disruptions to a
1743	      transfer session may cause not only resource waste (e.g. CPU
1744	      usage), but in some cases may result in financial loss on the part
1745	      of the gateway operator (e.g. fees charged by network).  Denial-
1746	      of-service attacks by third parties to a run of the protocol may
1747	      result in the termination of the current run (e.g. time-outs at
1748	      the SATP layer), and for new attempts to be conducted.

1750	   *  Dishonest gateways: The SATP protocol requires gateways to sign
1751	      messages related to the transfer layer, not only to provide
1752	      message source authentication and integrity but also to maintain
1753	      honesty on the part of the gateways.  Gateway-operators may take-
1754	      on legal and financial liabilities in certain jurisdictions by
1755	      digitally signing messages.  Dishonest gateways may intentionally
1756	      delay the delivery of certain messages or intentionally fail
1757	      (abort) the protocol run at certain crucial points [ARCH].  Two
1758	      such crucial points in the message flows are the following: (i)
1759	      the commit-final-msg, where the sender G1 asserts it has
1760	      extinguished (burned) the asset in the origin network, and (ii)
1761	      the ack-prepare-msg where the receiver gateway G2 asserts it is
1762	      ready to proceed with the final commitment.  If gateway G1
1763	      intentionally drops the commit-final-msg (commit-final) such that
1764	      gateway G2 times-out, then G2 may suffer financial loss due to
1765	      roll-back costs in network NW2.  Similarly, if G2 intentionally
1766	      drops the ack-prepare-msg to signal that it is ready to proceed
1767	      with the commitment (commit-ready), then gateway G1 may time-out
1768	      and terminate the protocol run, causing resource waste at G1.
1769	      Operators of gateways should utlize relevant tools to detect
1770	      possible dishonest behavior of certain gateways, and select to
1771	      have their gateways peer with other reliable gateways.

1773	   *  Protection of gateway keys: It is crucial to protect the
1774	      cryptographic keys utilized by gateways.  This includes keys for
1775	      secure session establishment (TLS1.3) and keys utilized for
1776	      signing SATP messages.  Loss of gateway keys may incur financial
1777	      loss on the part of the gateway-operator.  Implementation of
1778	      gateways should consider utilizing tamper-resistant hardware to
1779	      store and manage the relevant keys for gateways operational
1780	      functions.

1782	   *  Gateway identification: Mechanisms must be utilized to provide
1783	      unique identifiers to gateway implementations to ensure global
1784	      uniqueness and reachability.  Existing identification mechanisms
1785	      such a X509 certificates [RFC5280] and Verifiable Credentials
1786	      [W3CVC] may be applied for gateway identification.

1788	   *  Identification of networks: There needs to be mechanism for
1789	      gateways to declare or disclose the asset networks it current
1790	      serves.  Combined with strong gateway identification, this allows
1791	      remote gateways to quickly locate suitable gateways to peer with
1792	      for the purposes of asset transfers.
