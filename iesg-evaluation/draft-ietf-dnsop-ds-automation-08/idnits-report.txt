8	Operational Recommendations for DNSSEC Delegation Signer (DS) Automation
9	                   draft-ietf-dnsop-ds-automation-08

11	Abstract

13	   Enabling support for automatic acceptance of DNSSEC Delegation Signer
14	   (DS) parameters from the Child DNS operator (via RFCs 7344, 8078,
15	   9615) requires the parental agent, often a registry or registrar, to
16	   make a number of technical decisions around acceptance checks, error
17	   and success reporting, and multi-party issues such as concurrent
18	   updates.  This document describes recommendations about how these
19	   points are best addressed in practice.

106	1.  Introduction

108	   [RFC7344], [RFC8078], and [RFC9615] automate DNSSEC [RFC9364]
109	   delegation trust maintenance by having the child publish Child DS
110	   (CDS) and/or Child DNSKEY (CDNSKEY) records which indicate the
111	   delegation's desired DNSSEC parameters ("DS automation").

113	   Parental Agents using these protocols have to make a number of
114	   technical decisions relating to issues of acceptance checks, timing,
115	   error reporting, locks, etc.  Additionally, when using the
116	   registrant-registrar-registry (RRR) model (as is common amongst top-
117	   level domains), both the registrar and the registry can effect
118	   parent-side changes to the delegation.  In such a situation,
119	   additional opportunities for implementation differences arise.

121	   Not all existing DS automation deployments have made the same choices
122	   with respect to these questions, leading to somewhat inconsistent
123	   behavior.  From the perspective of a domain holder with domain names
124	   under various TLDs, this may be unexpected and confusing.

126	   In the following sections, operational questions are first raised and
127	   answered with the corresponding recommendations.  Each section is
128	   concluded with an analysis of its recommendations and related
129	   considerations.  A combined view of the recommendations from all
130	   sections is given in Appendix A.

132	   Readers are expected to be familiar with DNSSEC
133	   [RFC9364][RFC9615][RFC9859].

135	   The core issues addressed in the document are derived from
136	   Section 4.4 of [SAC126].  Readers are referred to this report for
137	   additional background.

139	1.1.  Requirements Notation

141	   The key words "MUST", "MUST NOT", "REQUIRED", "SHALL", "SHALL NOT",
142	   "SHOULD", "SHOULD NOT", "RECOMMENDED", "NOT RECOMMENDED", "MAY", and
143	   "OPTIONAL" in this document are to be interpreted as described in
144	   BCP 14 [RFC2119] [RFC8174] when, and only when, they appear in all
145	   capitals, as shown here.

147	2.  Terminology

149	   The term Parental Agent is used as defined in Section 1.1 of
150	   [RFC7344].  The document also uses terms defined in [RFC9499], in
151	   particular:

153	   *  DNS operator

155	   *  Registry

157	   *  Registrant

159	   *  Registrar

161	   In addition, the document makes use of the following terms:

163	   Child zone:  DNS zone whose delegation is in the Parent zone.

165	   Child (DNS operator):  DNS operator responsible for a Child zone.

167	   Parent zone:  DNS zone that holds a delegation for a Child zone.

169	   Parent:  The operator responsible for a Parent zone and, thus,
170	      involved with the maintenance of the delegation's DNSSEC
171	      parameters (in particular, the acceptance of these parameters and
172	      the publication of corresponding DS records).

174	   RRR Model:  The registrant-registrar-registry (RRR) interaction
175	      framework, where registrants interact with a registrar to register
176	      and manage domain names, and registrars interact with the domain's
177	      registry for the provision and management of domain names on the
178	      registrant's behalf.  This model is common amongst TLDs.

180	3.  Recommendations for Deployments of DS Automation

182	   The guidelines for deploying DS automation set out in this document
183	   are meant to achieve more uniform treatment across suffixes —
184	   minimizing user surprise and providing baseline safety and uniformity
185	   of behavior.  They are also intended to prevent disruption of DNS and
186	   DNSSEC functionality.  At a minimum, compliance with this RFC
187	   requires support for both DNSSEC bootstrapping [RFC9615] and
188	   subsequent updates [RFC7344], [RFC8078] under the implementation
189	   guidance below.

191	   The recommendations optimize interoperability and safety.  In certain
192	   cases, local policy may take precedence, such as when a registry is
193	   subjected to national cryptographic policy requirements or performs
194	   out-of-band verification of DS changes with a human for high-stake
195	   domains.  However, not following any requirements designated with the
196	   "SHOULD" key word will generally lead to undesirable effects of
197	   ambiguity and interoperability issues.  When implementing these
198	   recommendations, operators have to carefully weigh whether any
199	   particular deviation is justified in their particular context.

201	   Registries with additional requirements on DS update checks MAY
202	   implement any additional checks in line with local policy.

204	4.  Acceptance Checks and Safety Measures

206	   This section provides recommendations to address the following
207	   operational questions:

209	   *  What kind of acceptance checks should be performed on DS
210	      parameters?

212	   *  Should these checks be performed upon acceptance or also
213	      continually when in place?

215	   *  How do TTLs and caching impact DS provisioning?  How important is
216	      timing in a child key change?

218	   *  Are parameters for DS automation best conveyed as CDNSKEY or CDS
219	      records, or both?

221	4.1.  Recommendations

223	   1.  Entities performing automated DS maintenance MUST verify:

225	       a.  the unambiguous intent of each DS update request as per
226	           [I-D.ietf-dnsop-cds-consistency], by checking its consistency
227	           both

229	           *  between any published CDS and CDNSKEY records, and

231	           *  across all authoritative nameservers in the delegation,

233	           and

235	       b.  that the resulting DS record set would allow continued DNSSEC
236	           validation if deployed,

238	       and cancel the update if the verifications do not succeed.

240	   2.  Parent-side entities (such as registries) SHOULD reduce a DS
241	       record set's TTL to a value between 5–15 minutes when a new set
242	       of records is published, and restore the normal TTL value at a
243	       later occasion (but not before the previous DS RRset's TTL has
244	       expired).

246	   3.  DNS operators SHOULD publish both CDNSKEY and CDS records, and
247	       follow best practice for the choice of hash digest type
248	       [DS-IANA].

250	4.2.  Analysis

252	4.2.1.  Continuity of Resolution

254	   To maintain the basic resolution function, it is important to avoid
255	   the deployment of flawed DS record sets in the parent zone.  It is
256	   therefore desirable for the Parent to verify that the DS record set
257	   resulting from an automated (or even manual) update does not break
258	   DNSSEC validation if deployed, and otherwise cancel the update.

260	   This is best achieved by:

262	   1.  verifying that consistent CDS/CDNSKEY responses are served by all
263	       of the delegation's nameservers [I-D.ietf-dnsop-cds-consistency];

265	   2.  verifying that the resulting DS Resource Record set (RRset) does
266	       not break the delegation if applied ([RFC7344], Section 4.1),
267	       i.e., it provides at least one valid path for validators to use
268	       ([RFC6840], Section 5.11).  This is the case if the child's
269	       DNSKEY RRset has a valid RRSIG signature from a key that is
270	       referenced by at least one DS record, with the digest type and
271	       signing algorithm values designated as "RECOMMENDED" or "MUST" in
272	       the "Use for DNSSEC Validation" columns of the relevant IANA
273	       registries ([DS-IANA] and [DNSKEY-IANA]).  Note that these checks
274	       need not be enforced when provisioning DS records manually in
275	       order to enable the use other digest types or algorithms for
276	       potentially non-interoperable purposes.

278	   Even without an update being requested, Parents may occasionally
279	   check whether the current DS contents would still be acceptable if
280	   they were newly submitted in CDS/CDNSKEY form (see Section 4).  Any
281	   failures — such as a missing DNSKEY due to improper rollover timing
282	   ([RFC6781], Section 4.1), or changed algorithm requirements — can
283	   then be communicated in line with Section 5, without altering or
284	   removing the existing DS RRset.

286	4.2.2.  TTLs and Caching

288	   To further reduce the impact of any misconfigured DS record set — be
289	   it from automated or from manual provisioning — the option to quickly
290	   roll back the delegation's DNSSEC parameters is of great importance.
291	   This is achieved by setting a comparatively low TTL on the DS record
292	   set in the parent domain, at the cost of reduced resiliency against
293	   nameserver unreachability due to the earlier expiration of cached
294	   records.  The availability risk can be mitigated by limiting such
295	   TTLs to a brief time period after a change to the DS configuration,
296	   during which rollbacks are most likely to occur.

298	   Registries therefore should significantly lower the DS RRset's TTL
299	   for some time following bootstrapping or an update.  Pragmatic values
300	   for the reduced TTL value range between 5–15 minutes.  Such low TTLs
301	   might be expected to cause increased load on the corresponding
302	   authoritative nameservers; however, recent measurements have
303	   demonstrated them to have negligible impact on the overall load of a
304	   registry's authoritative nameserver infrastructure [LowTTL].

306	   The reduction should be in effect at least for a couple of days and
307	   until the previous DS record set has expired from caches, that is,
308	   the period during which the low-TTL is applied typically will
309	   significantly exceed the normal TTL value.  When using the Extensible
310	   Provisioning Protocol (EPP) [RFC5730], the domain <info> command
311	   described in Section 2.1.1.2 of [RFC9803] is available for
312	   advertising the server's TTL policy.

314	   While this approach enables quick rollbacks, timing of the desired DS
315	   update process itself is largely governed by the previous DS RRset's
316	   TTL, and therefore does not generally benefit from an overall speed-
317	   up.  Note also that nothing is gained from first lowering the TTL of
318	   the old DS RRset: such an additional step would, in fact, require
319	   another wait period while resolver caches adjust.  For the sake of
320	   completeness, there likewise is no point to increasing any DS TTL
321	   values beyond their normal value.

323	4.2.3.  CDS vs. CDNSKEY

325	   DS records can be generated from information provided either in DS
326	   format (CDS) or in DNSKEY format (CDNSKEY).  While the format of CDS
327	   records is identical to that of DS records (so the record data be
328	   taken verbatim), generation of a DS record from CDNSKEY information
329	   involves computing a hash.

331	   Whether a Parent processes CDS or CDNSKEY records depends on their
332	   preference:

334	   *  Processing (and storing) CDNSKEY information allows the Parent to
335	      control the choice of hash algorithms.  The Parent may then
336	      unilaterally regenerate DS records with a different choice of hash
337	      algorithm(s) whenever deemed appropriate.

339	   *  Processing CDS information allows the Child DNS operator to
340	      control the hash digest type used in DS records, enabling the
341	      Child DNS operator to deploy (for example) experimental hash
342	      digests and removing the need for registry-side changes when
343	      additional digest types become available.

345	   The need to make a choice in the face of this dichotomy is not
346	   specific to DS automation: even when DNSSEC parameters are relayed to
347	   the Parent through conventional channels, the Parent has to make some
348	   choice about which format(s) to accept.

350	   As there exists no protocol for Child DNS operators to discover a
351	   Parent's input format preference, it is best for interoperability to
352	   publish both CDNSKEY as well as CDS records, in line with Section 5
353	   of [RFC7344].  The choice of hash digest type should follow current
354	   best practice [DS-IANA].

356	   Publishing the same information in two different formats is not
357	   ideal.  Still, it is much less complex and costly than burdening the
358	   Child DNS operator with discovering each Parent's current policy.
359	   Also, it is very easily automated.  Operators should ensure that
360	   published RRsets are consistent with each other.

362	   If both RRsets are published, Parents are expected to verify
363	   consistency between them by verifying that they refer to the same set
364	   of keys [I-D.ietf-dnsop-cds-consistency].  By not second-guessing
365	   inconsistencies (such as by RRset recency) and instead rejecting
366	   them, responsibility to clearly express each update request is placed
367	   on the Child DNS operator.

369	   CDS records need only be considered for CDNSKEY consistency when
370	   their digest type field is designated as "MUST" in the "Implement for
371	   DNSSEC Delegation" column of the "Digest Algorithms" registry
372	   [DS-IANA].  Consistency of records with other digest types need not
373	   be verified, especially when the digest type is unsupported; such
374	   records can be ignored.  Note that this does not imply a restriction
375	   on the DS hash digest types: if no inconsistencies are found, the
376	   parent can publish DS records with whatever digest type(s) it
377	   prefers.

379	5.  Reporting and Transparency

381	   This section provides recommendations to address the following
382	   operational question:

384	   *  Should a failed (or even successful) DS update trigger a
385	      notification to anyone?

387	5.1.  Recommendations

389	   1.  For certain DS updates (see analysis (Section 5.2)) and for DS
390	       deactivation, relevant points of contact known to the parent-side
391	       entity (registry or registrar) SHOULD be notified.

393	   2.  For error conditions, the child DNS operator and the domain's
394	       technical contact (if applicable) SHOULD be notified first.  The
395	       registrant SHOULD NOT be notified unless the problem persists for
396	       a prolonged amount of time (e.g., three days).

398	   3.  Child DNS operators SHOULD be notified of errors using a report
399	       query [RFC9567] to the agent domain as described in Section 4 of
400	       [RFC9859].  Notifications to humans (domain holder) will be
401	       performed in accordance with the communication preferences
402	       established with the parent-side entity.  The same condition
403	       SHOULD NOT be reported unnecessarily frequently to the same
404	       recipient.

406	   4.  In the RRR model, registries performing DS automation SHOULD
407	       inform the registrar of any DS record changes via the EPP Change
408	       Poll Extension [RFC8590] or a similar channel.

410	   5.  The currently active DS configuration SHOULD be made accessible
411	       to the registrant (or their designated party) through the
412	       customer portal available for domain management.  The DS update
413	       history MAY be made available in the same way.

415	5.2.  Analysis

417	   When accepting or rejecting a DS update, it cannot be assumed that
418	   relevant parties are aware of what's happening.  For example, a
419	   registrar may not know when an automatic DS update is performed by
420	   the registry.  Similarly, a Child DNS operator may not be aware when
421	   their CDS/CDNSKEY RRsets are out of sync across nameservers, causing
422	   them to be ignored.

424	   To help involved parties act appropriately and in a timely manner,
425	   entities performing automated DS maintenance should report on
426	   conditions they encounter.  The following success situations may be
427	   of particular interest:

429	   1.  A DS RRset has been provisioned

431	       a.  manually;

433	       b.  due to commencing DS automation (either via DNSSEC
434	           bootstrapping, or for the first time after a manual change;
435	           see Section 7);

437	       c.  automatically, as an update to an existing DS RRset that had
438	           itself been automatically provisioned.

440	   2.  The DS RRset has been removed

442	       a.  manually;

444	       b.  automatically, using a delete signal ([RFC8078], Section 4).

446	   In addition, there are error conditions worthy of being reported:

448	   3.  A pending DS update cannot be applied due to an error condition.
449	       There are various scenarios where an automated DS update might
450	       have been requested, but can't be fulfilled.  These include:

452	       a.  The new DS record set would break validation/resolution or is
453	           not acceptable to the Parent for some other reason (see
454	           Section 4).

456	       b.  A lock prevents DS automation (see Section 6).

458	   4.  No DS update is due, but it was determined that the Child zone is
459	       no longer compatible with the existing DS record set (e.g., DS
460	       RRset only references non-existing keys).

462	   In these latter two cases, the entity performing DS automation would
463	   be justified to attempt communicating the situation.  Potential
464	   recipients are:

466	   *  Child DNS operator, preferably by making a report query [RFC9567]
467	      to the agent domain listed in the EDNS0 Report-Channel option of
468	      the DS update notification that triggered the DS update
469	      ([RFC9859], Section 4), or else via email to the address contained
470	      in the child zone's SOA RNAME field (see Sections 3.3.13 and 8 of
471	      [RFC1035]);

473	   *  Registrar (if DS automation is performed by the registry);

475	   *  Registrant (domain holder; in non-technical language, such as
476	      "DNSSEC security for your domain has been enabled and will be
477	      maintained automatically") or technical contact, in accordance
478	      with the communication preferences established with the parent-
479	      side entity.

481	   For manual updates (case 1a), commencing DS automation (case 1b), and
482	   deactivating DNSSEC (case 2), it seems worthwhile to notify both the
483	   domain's technical contact (if applicable) and the registrant.  This
484	   will typically lead to one notification during normal operation of a
485	   domain.  (Case 1c, the regular operation of automation, is not an
486	   interesting condition to report to a human.)

488	   For error conditions (cases 3 and 4), the registrant need not always
489	   be involved.  It seems advisable to first notify the domain's
490	   technical contact and the DNS operator serving the affected Child
491	   zone, and only if the problem persists for a prolonged amount of time
492	   (e.g., three days), notify the registrant.

494	   When the RRR model is used and the registry performs DS automation,
495	   the registrar should always stay informed of any DS record changes,
496	   e.g., via the EPP Change Poll Extension [RFC8590].

498	   Overly frequent reporting of the same condition to the same recipient
499	   is discouraged (e.g., no more than twice in a row).  For example,
500	   when CDS and CDNSKEY records are inconsistent and prevent DS
501	   initialization, the registrant may be notified twice.  Additional
502	   notifications may be sent with some back-off mechanism (in increasing
503	   intervals).

505	   The registrant (or their designated party) should be able to retrieve
506	   the current DS configuration through the customer portal available
507	   for domain management.  Ideally, the history of DS updates would also
508	   be available.  However, due to the associated state requirements and
509	   the lack of direct operational impact, implementation of this is
510	   optional.

512	   For troubleshooting, dispute resolution, and post-incident analysis,
513	   it is instrumental for the Parental Agent to retain structured
514	   records of DS automation decisions, including timestamp, triggering
515	   CDS/CDNSKEY RRsets, notification channel, authoritative nameservers
516	   consulted, verification results, decision outcome, and the applied DS
517	   RRset or cancellation reason.

519	6.  Registration Locks

521	   This section provides recommendations to address the following
522	   operational question:

524	   *  How does DS automation interact with other registration state
525	      parameters, such as registration locks?

527	6.1.  Recommendations

529	   1.  To secure ongoing operations, automated DS maintenance MUST NOT
530	       be suspended based on a registrar update lock alone (such as EPP
531	       status clientUpdateProhibited [RFC5731]).

533	   2.  When performed by the registry, automated DS maintenance MUST NOT
534	       be suspended based on a registry update lock alone (such as EPP
535	       status serverUpdateProhibited [RFC5731]).

537	6.2.  Analysis

539	   Registries and registrars can set various types of locks for domain
540	   registrations, usually upon the registrant's request.  An overview of
541	   standardized locks using EPP, for example, is given in Section 2.3 of
542	   [RFC5731].  Some registries may offer additional (or other) types of
543	   locks whose meaning and set/unset mechanisms are defined according to
544	   a proprietary policy.

546	   While some locks clearly should have no impact on DS automation (such
547	   as transfer or deletion locks), other types of locks, in particular
548	   "update locks", deserve a closer analysis.

550	6.2.1.  Registrar vs. Registry Lock

552	   A registrar-side update lock (such as clientUpdateProhibited in EPP)
553	   protects against various types of accidental or malicious change
554	   (like unintended changes through the registrar's customer portal).
555	   Its security model does not prevent the registrar's (nor the
556	   registry's) actions.  This is because a registrar-side lock can be
557	   removed by the registrar without an out-of-band interaction.

559	   Under such a security model, no tangible security benefit is gained
560	   by preventing automated DS maintenance based on a registrar lock
561	   alone, while preventing it would make maintenance needlessly
562	   difficult.  It therefore seems reasonable not to suspend automation
563	   when such a lock is present.

565	   When a registry-side update lock is in place, the registrar cannot
566	   apply any changes (for security or delinquency or other reasons).
567	   However, it does not protect against changes made by the registry
568	   itself.  This is exemplified by the serverUpdateProhibited EPP
569	   status, which demands only that the registrar's "[r]equests to update
570	   the object [...] MUST be rejected" (Section 2.3 of [RFC5731]).  This
571	   type of lock therefore precludes DS automation by the registrar,
572	   while registry-side automation may continue.

574	   DS automation by the registry further is consistent with Section 2.3
575	   of [RFC5731], which explicitly notes that an EPP server (registry)
576	   may override status values set by an EPP client (registrar), subject
577	   to local server policies.  The risk that DS changes from registry-
578	   side DS automation might go unnoticed by the registrar is mitigated
579	   by sending change notifications to the registrar; see Recommendation
580	   4 of Section 5.

582	6.2.2.  Detailed Rationale

584	   Pre-DNSSEC, it was possible for a registration to be set up once,
585	   then locked and left alone (no maintenance required).  With DNSSEC
586	   comes a change to this operational model: the configuration may have
587	   to be maintained in order to remain secure and operational.  For
588	   example, the Child DNS operator may switch to another signing
589	   algorithm if the previous one is no longer deemed appropriate, or
590	   roll its Secure Entry Point (SEP) key for other reasons.  Such
591	   changes entail updating the delegation's DS records.

593	   If authenticated, these operations do not qualify as accidental or
594	   malicious change, but as legitimate and normal activity for securing
595	   ongoing operation.  The CDS/CDNSKEY method provides an automatic,
596	   authenticated means to convey DS update requests.  The resulting DS
597	   update is subject to the parent's acceptance checks; in particular,
598	   it is not applied when it would break the delegation (see Section 4).

600	   Given that registrar locks protect against unintended changes (such
601	   as through the customer portal) while not preventing actions done by
602	   the registrar (or the registry) themself, such a lock is not suitable
603	   for defending against actions performed illegitimately by the
604	   registrar or registry (e.g., due to compromise).  Any attack on the
605	   registration data that is feasible in the presence of a registrar
606	   lock is also feasible regardless of whether DS maintenance is done
607	   automatically; in other words, DS automation is orthogonal to the
608	   attack vector that a registrar lock protects against.

610	   Considering that automated DS updates are required to be
611	   authenticated and validated for correctness, it thus appears that
612	   honoring such requests, while in the registrant's interest, comes
613	   with no additional associated risk.  Suspending automated DS
614	   maintenance therefore does not seem justified.

616	   Following this line of thought, at the time of document writing some
617	   registries (e.g., .ch/.cz/.li) perform automated DS maintenance even
618	   when an "update lock" is in place.  Registries offering proprietary
619	   locks should carefully consider for each lock whether its scope
620	   warrants suspension.

622	   In case of a domain not yet secured with DNSSEC, automatic DS
623	   initialization is not required to maintain ongoing operation;
624	   however, authenticated DNSSEC bootstrapping [RFC9615] might be
625	   requested.  Besides being in the interest of security, the fact that
626	   a Child is requesting DS initialization through an authenticated
627	   method expresses the registrant's intent to have the delegation
628	   secured.

630	   Further, some domains are equipped with an update lock by default.
631	   Not honoring DNSSEC bootstrapping requests then imposes an additional
632	   burden on the registrant, who has to unlock and relock the domain in
633	   order to facilitate DS provisioning after registration.  This is a
634	   needless cost especially for large domain portfolios.  It is also
635	   unexpected, as the registrant already has arranged for the necessary
636	   CDS/CDNSKEY records to be published.  DS initialization and rollovers
637	   therefore should be treated the same way with respect to locks.

639	7.  Multiple Submitting Parties

641	   This section provides recommendations to address the following
642	   operational questions:

644	   *  How are conflicts resolved when DS parameters are accepted through
645	      multiple channels (e.g., via a conventional channel and via
646	      automation)?

648	   *  In case both the registry and the registrar are automating DS
649	      updates, how to resolve potential collisions?

651	7.1.  Recommendations

653	   1.  Registries and registrars MUST provide another (e.g., manual)
654	       channel for DS maintenance in order to enable recovery when the
655	       Child has lost access to its signing key(s).  This out-of-band
656	       channel is also needed when a DNS operator does not support DS
657	       automation or refuses to cooperate.

659	   2.  DS update requests SHOULD be executed immediately after
660	       verification of their authenticity, regardless of whether they
661	       are received in-band or via an out-of-band channel.

663	   3.  When processing a CDS/CDNSKEY "delete" signal to remove the
664	       entire DS record set ([RFC8078], Section 4), DS automation MUST
665	       NOT be suspended.  For all other removal requests (such as when
666	       received via EPP or a web form), DS automation SHOULD be
667	       suspended until a new DS record set has been provisioned, in
668	       order to prevent accidental re-initialization when the registrant
669	       intended to disable DNSSEC.

671	   4.  Whenever a non-empty DS record set is provisioned, through
672	       whichever channel, DS automation SHOULD NOT (or no longer) be
673	       suspended (including after an earlier removal).

675	   5.  In the RRR model, a registry SHOULD NOT automatically initialize
676	       DS records when it is known that the registrar does not provide a
677	       way for the domain holder to later disable DNSSEC.  If the
678	       registrar has declared that it performs automated DS maintenance,
679	       the registry SHOULD publish the registrar's [RFC9859]
680	       notification endpoint (if applicable) and refrain from registry-
681	       side DS automation.

683	7.2.  Analysis

685	   In the RRR model, there are multiple channels through which DS
686	   parameters can be accepted:

688	   *  The registry can retrieve information about an intended DS update
689	      automatically from the Child DNS operator and apply the update
690	      directly;

692	   *  The registrar can retrieve the same and relay it to the registry;

694	   *  The registrar can obtain the information from the registrant
695	      through another channel (such as a non-automated "manual update"
696	      via webform submission), and relay it to the registry.

698	   There are several considerations in this context, as discussed in the
699	   following subsections.

701	7.2.1.  Necessity of Non-automatic Updates

703	   Under special circumstances, it may be necessary to perform a non-
704	   automatic DS update.  One important example is when the key used for
705	   authentication of DS updates is destroyed: in this case, an automatic
706	   key rollover is impossible as the Child DNS operator can no longer
707	   authenticate the associated information.  Another example is when
708	   several providers are involved, but one no longer cooperates (e.g.,
709	   when removing a provider from a multi-provider setup).  Disabling
710	   manual DS management interfaces is therefore strongly discouraged.

712	   Similarly, when the registrar is known to not support DNSSEC (or to
713	   lack a DS provisioning interface), it seems adequate for registries
714	   to not perform automated DS maintenance, in order to prevent
715	   situations in which a misconfigured delegation cannot be repaired by
716	   the registrant.

718	7.2.2.  Impact of Non-automatic Updates: When to Suspend Automation

720	   When an out-of-band (e.g., manual) DS update is performed while CDS/
721	   CDNSKEY records referencing the previous DS RRset's keys are present,
722	   the delegation's DS records may be reset to their previous state at
723	   the next run of the automation process.  This section discusses in
724	   which situations it is appropriate to suspend DS automation after
725	   such a non-automatic update.

727	   One option is to suspend DS automation after a manual DS update, but
728	   only until a resumption signal is observed.  In the past, it was
729	   proposed that seeing an updated SOA serial in the child zone may
730	   serve as a resumption signal.  However, as any arbitrary modification
731	   of zone contents — including the regular updating of DNSSEC signature
732	   validity timestamps — typically causes a change in SOA serial,
733	   resumption of DS automation after a serial change comes with a high
734	   risk of surprise.  Additional issues arise if nameservers have
735	   different serial offsets (e.g., in a multi-provider setup).  This
736	   practice therefore is NOT RECOMMENDED.

738	   Note also that "automatic rollback" due to old CDS/CDNSKEY RRsets can
739	   only occur if they are signed with a key authorized by one of new DS
740	   records.  Acceptance checks described in Section 4 further ensure
741	   that updates do not break validation.

743	   Removal of a DS record set is triggered either through a CDS/CDNSKEY
744	   "delete" signal observed by the party performing the automation
745	   ([RFC8078], Section 4), or by receiving a removal request out-of-band
746	   (e.g., via EPP or a web form).  In the first case, it is useful to
747	   keep automation active for the delegation in question, to facilitate
748	   later DS bootstrapping.  In the second case, it is likely that the
749	   registrant intends to disable DNSSEC for the domain, and DS
750	   automation is best suspended (until a new DS record is provisioned
751	   somehow).

753	   One may ask how a registry can know whether a removal request
754	   received via EPP was the result of the registrar observing a CDS/
755	   CDNSKEY "delete" signal.  It turns out that the registry does not
756	   need to know that; in fact, the advice works out nicely regardless of
757	   who does the automation:

759	   a.  Only registry: If the registry performs automation, then the
760	       registry will consider any request received from the registrar as
761	       out-of-band (in the context of this automation).  When such
762	       requests demand removal of the entire DS record set, the registry
763	       therefore should suspend automation.

765	   b.  Only registrar: The registrar can always distinguish between
766	       removal requests obtained from a CDS/CDNSKEY "delete" signal and
767	       other registrant requests, and suspend automation as appropriate.

769	   c.  In the (undesirable) case that both parties automate, there are
770	       two cases:

772	       *  If the registrant submits a manual removal request to the
773	          registrar, it is out-of-band from the registrar perspective
774	          (e.g., web form), and also for the registry (e.g., EPP).  As a
775	          consequence, both will suspend automation (which is the
776	          correct result).

778	       *  If a CDS/CDNSKEY "delete" signal causes the registrar to
779	          request DS removal from the registry, then the registry will
780	          suspend automation (because the removal request is received
781	          out-of-band, such as via EPP).  This is independent of whether
782	          the registry's automation has already seen the signal.  The
783	          registrar, however, will be aware of the in-band nature of the
784	          request and not suspend automation (which is also the correct
785	          result).

787	       As a side effect, this works towards avoiding redundant
788	       automation at the registry.

790	   All in all:

792	   *  It is advisable to generally not suspend in-band DS automation
793	      when an out-of-band DS update has occurred.

795	   *  An exception from this rule is when the entire DS record set was
796	      removed through an out-of-band request, in which case the
797	      registrant likely wants to disable DNSSEC for the domain.  DS
798	      automation should then be suspended so that automatic re-
799	      initialization (bootstrapping) does not occur.

801	   *  In all other cases, any properly authenticated DS updates
802	      received, including through an automated method, are to be
803	      considered as the current intent of the domain holder.

805	7.2.3.  Concurrent Automatic Updates

807	   When the RRR model is used, there is a potential for collision if
808	   both the registry and the registrar are automating DS provisioning by
809	   scanning the child for CDS/CDNSKEY records.  No disruptive
810	   consequences are expected if both parties perform DS automation.  An
811	   exception is when during a key rollover, registry and registrar see
812	   different versions of the Child's DS update requests, such as when
813	   CDS/CDNSKEY records are retrieved from different vantage points.
814	   Although unlikely due to Recommendation 1a of Section 4, this may
815	   lead to flapping of DS updates.  However, it is not expected to be
816	   harmful as either DS RRset will allow for the validation function to
817	   continue to work, as ensured by Recommendation 1b of Section 4.  The
818	   effect subsides as the Child's state eventually becomes consistent
819	   (roughly, within the child's replication delay); any flapping until
820	   then will be a minor nuisance only.

822	   The issue disappears entirely when scanning is replaced by
823	   notifications that trigger DS maintenance through one party's
824	   designated endpoint [RFC9859], and can otherwise be mitigated if the
825	   registry and registrar agree that only one of them will perform
826	   scanning.

828	   As a standard aspect of key rollovers [RFC6781], the Child DNS
829	   operator is expected to monitor propagation of Child zone updates to
830	   all authoritative nameserver instances, and only proceed to the next
831	   step once replication has succeeded everywhere and the DS record set
832	   was subsequently updated (and in no case before the DS RRset's TTL
833	   has passed).  Any breakage resulting from improper timing on the
834	   Child side is outside of the Parent's sphere of influence, and thus
835	   cannot be handled with only parent-side changes.

974	Appendix A.  Recommendations Overview

976	   For ease of review and referencing, the recommendations from this
977	   document are reproduced here without further comment.  For background
978	   and analysis, refer to Sections 4–7.

980	A.1.  Acceptance Checks and Safety Measures

982	   1.  Entities performing automated DS maintenance MUST verify:

984	       a.  the unambiguous intent of each DS update request as per
985	           [I-D.ietf-dnsop-cds-consistency], by checking its consistency
986	           both

988	           *  between any published CDS and CDNSKEY records, and

990	           *  across all authoritative nameservers in the delegation,

992	           and

994	       b.  that the resulting DS record set would allow continued DNSSEC
995	           validation if deployed,

997	       and cancel the update if the verifications do not succeed.

999	   2.  Parent-side entities (such as registries) SHOULD reduce a DS
1000	       record set's TTL to a value between 5–15 minutes when a new set
1001	       of records is published, and restore the normal TTL value at a
1002	       later occasion (but not before the previous DS RRset's TTL has
1003	       expired).

1005	   3.  DNS operators SHOULD publish both CDNSKEY and CDS records, and
1006	       follow best practice for the choice of hash digest type
1007	       [DS-IANA].

1009	A.2.  Reporting and Transparency

1011	   1.  For certain DS updates (see analysis (Section 5.2)) and for DS
1012	       deactivation, relevant points of contact known to the parent-side
1013	       entity (registry or registrar) SHOULD be notified.

1015	   2.  For error conditions, the child DNS operator and the domain's
1016	       technical contact (if applicable) SHOULD be notified first.  The
1017	       registrant SHOULD NOT be notified unless the problem persists for
1018	       a prolonged amount of time (e.g., three days).

1020	   3.  Child DNS operators SHOULD be notified of errors using a report
1021	       query [RFC9567] to the agent domain as described in Section 4 of
1022	       [RFC9859].  Notifications to humans (domain holder) will be
1023	       performed in accordance with the communication preferences
1024	       established with the parent-side entity.  The same condition
1025	       SHOULD NOT be reported unnecessarily frequently to the same
1026	       recipient.

1028	   4.  In the RRR model, registries performing DS automation SHOULD
1029	       inform the registrar of any DS record changes via the EPP Change
1030	       Poll Extension [RFC8590] or a similar channel.

1032	   5.  The currently active DS configuration SHOULD be made accessible
1033	       to the registrant (or their designated party) through the
1034	       customer portal available for domain management.  The DS update
1035	       history MAY be made available in the same way.

1037	A.3.  Registration Locks

1039	   1.  To secure ongoing operations, automated DS maintenance MUST NOT
1040	       be suspended based on a registrar update lock alone (such as EPP
1041	       status clientUpdateProhibited [RFC5731]).

1043	   2.  When performed by the registry, automated DS maintenance MUST NOT
1044	       be suspended based on a registry update lock alone (such as EPP
1045	       status serverUpdateProhibited [RFC5731]).

1047	A.4.  Multiple Submitting Parties

1049	   1.  Registries and registrars MUST provide another (e.g., manual)
1050	       channel for DS maintenance in order to enable recovery when the
1051	       Child has lost access to its signing key(s).  This out-of-band
1052	       channel is also needed when a DNS operator does not support DS
1053	       automation or refuses to cooperate.

1055	   2.  DS update requests SHOULD be executed immediately after
1056	       verification of their authenticity, regardless of whether they
1057	       are received in-band or via an out-of-band channel.

1059	   3.  When processing a CDS/CDNSKEY "delete" signal to remove the
1060	       entire DS record set ([RFC8078], Section 4), DS automation MUST
1061	       NOT be suspended.  For all other removal requests (such as when
1062	       received via EPP or a web form), DS automation SHOULD be
1063	       suspended until a new DS record set has been provisioned, in
1064	       order to prevent accidental re-initialization when the registrant
1065	       intended to disable DNSSEC.

1067	   4.  Whenever a non-empty DS record set is provisioned, through
1068	       whichever channel, DS automation SHOULD NOT (or no longer) be
1069	       suspended (including after an earlier removal).

1071	   5.  In the RRR model, a registry SHOULD NOT automatically initialize
1072	       DS records when it is known that the registrar does not provide a
1073	       way for the domain holder to later disable DNSSEC.  If the
1074	       registrar has declared that it performs automated DS maintenance,
1075	       the registry SHOULD publish the registrar's [RFC9859]
1076	       notification endpoint (if applicable) and refrain from registry-
1077	       side DS automation.
