12	                draft-ietf-tls-deprecate-obsolete-kex-06

14	Abstract

16	   For (D)TLS 1.2, this document deprecates the use of two key
17	   exchanges, namely Diffie-Hellman over a finite field and RSA, and it
18	   discourages the use of static elliptic curve Diffie-Hellman cipher
19	   suites.

21	   These prescriptions apply only to (D)TLS 1.2 since (D)TLS 1.0 and TLS
22	   1.1 are deprecated by RFC 8996 and (D)TLS 1.3 either does not use the
23	   affected algorithm or does not share the relevant configuration
24	   options.  (There is no DTLS version 1.1.)

26	   This document updates RFCs 9325, 4346, 5246, 4162, 6347, 5932, 5288,
27	   6209, 6367, 8422, 5289, 5469, 4785, 4279, 5487, 6655, and 7905.

83	1.  Introduction

85	   (D)TLS 1.2 supports a variety of key exchange algorithms, including
86	   RSA, Diffie-Hellman over a finite field, and elliptic curve Diffie-
87	   Hellman (ECDH).

89	   Diffie-Hellman key exchange, over any group, comes in ephemeral and
90	   non-ephemeral varieties.  Non-ephemeral DH algorithms use static DH
91	   public keys included in the authenticating peer's certificate; see
92	   [RFC4492] for discussion.  In contrast, ephemeral DH algorithms use
93	   ephemeral DH public keys sent in the handshake and authenticated by
94	   the peer's certificate.  Ephemeral and non-ephemeral finite field DH
95	   algorithms are called DHE and DH (or FFDHE and FFDH), respectively,
96	   and ephemeral and non-ephemeral elliptic curve DH algorithms are
97	   called ECDHE and ECDH, respectively [RFC4492].

99	   In general, non-ephemeral cipher suites are not recommended due to
100	   their lack of forward secrecy.  Moreover, as demonstrated by the
101	   [Raccoon] attack on finite-field DH, public key reuse, either via
102	   non-ephemeral cipher suites or reused keys with ephemeral cipher
103	   suites, can lead to timing side channels that may leak connection
104	   secrets.  For elliptic curve DH, invalid curve attacks similarly
105	   exploit secret reuse in order to break security [ICA], further
106	   demonstrating the risk of reusing public keys.  While both side
107	   channels can be avoided in implementations, experience shows that in
108	   practice, implementations may fail to thwart such attacks due to the
109	   complexity and number of the required mitigations.

111	   Additionally, RSA key exchange suffers from security problems that
112	   are independent of implementation choices as well as problems that
113	   stem purely from the difficulty of implementing security
114	   countermeasures correctly.

116	   At a rough glance, the problems affecting FFDHE in (D)TLS 1.2 are as
117	   follows:

119	   1.  FFDHE suffers from interoperability problems because there is no
120	       mechanism for negotiating the group, and some implementations
121	       only support small group sizes (see [RFC7919], Section 1).

123	   2.  FFDHE groups may have small subgroups, which enables several
124	       attacks [subgroups].  When presented with a custom, non-
125	       standardized FFDHE group, a handshaking client cannot practically
126	       verify that the group chosen by the server does not suffer from
127	       this problem.  There is also no mechanism for such handshakes to
128	       fall back to other key exchange parameters that are acceptable to
129	       the client.  Custom FFDHE groups are widespread (as a result of
130	       advice based on [weak-dh]).  Therefore, clients cannot simply
131	       reject handshakes that present custom, and thus potentially
132	       dangerous, groups.

134	   3.  In practice, some operators use 1024-bit FFDHE groups since this
135	       is the maximum size that ensures wide support (see [RFC7919],
136	       Section 1).  This size leaves only a small security margin vs.
137	       the current discrete log record, which stands at 795 bits
138	       [DLOG795].

140	   4.  Expanding on the previous point, just a handful of very large
141	       computations allow an attacker to cheaply decrypt a relatively
142	       large fraction of FFDHE traffic (namely, traffic encrypted using
143	       particular standardized groups) [weak-dh].

145	   5.  When secrets are not fully ephemeral, FFDHE suffers from the
146	       [Raccoon] side channel attack.  (Note that FFDH is inherently
147	       vulnerable to the Raccoon attack unless constant-time mitigations
148	       are employed.)

150	   The problems affecting RSA key exchange in (D)TLS 1.2 are as follows:

152	   1.  RSA key exchange offers no forward secrecy, by construction.

154	   2.  RSA key exchange may be vulnerable to Bleichenbacher's attack
155	       [BLEI].  Experience shows that variants of this attack arise
156	       every few years because implementing the relevant countermeasure
157	       correctly is difficult (see [ROBOT], [NEW-BLEI], [DROWN]).

159	   3.  In addition to the above point, there is no convenient mechanism
160	       in (D)TLS 1.2 for the domain separation of keys.  Therefore, a
161	       single endpoint that is vulnerable to Bleichenbacher's attack
162	       would affect all endpoints sharing the same RSA key (see [XPROT],
163	       [DROWN]).

165	   This document updates [RFC9325], [RFC4346], [RFC5246], [RFC4162],
166	   [RFC6347], [RFC5932], [RFC5288], [RFC6209], [RFC6367], [RFC8422],
167	   [RFC5289], [RFC4785], [RFC4279], [RFC5487], [RFC6655], [RFC7905] and
168	   [RFC5469] to remediate the above problems.

170	   [RFC9325] contains the latest IETF recommendations for users of the
171	   (D)TLS protocol (and specifically, (D)TLS 1.2) and this document
172	   supersedes it in several points.  Appendix F details the exact
173	   differences.  All other recommendations of the BCP document remain
174	   valid.

184	2.  Non-Ephemeral Diffie-Hellman

186	   Clients MUST NOT offer and servers MUST NOT select non-ephemeral FFDH
187	   cipher suites in (D)TLS 1.2 connections.  (Note that (D)TLS 1.0 and
188	   TLS 1.1 are deprecated by [RFC8996] and (D)TLS 1.3 does not support
189	   FFDH [I-D.ietf-tls-rfc8446bis][RFC9147].)  This includes all cipher
190	   suites listed in the table in Appendix A.

192	   Clients SHOULD NOT offer and servers SHOULD NOT select non-ephemeral
193	   ECDH cipher suites in (D)TLS 1.2 connections.  (This requirement is
194	   already present in [RFC9325].  Note that (D)TLS 1.0 and TLS 1.1 are
195	   deprecated by [RFC8996] and (D)TLS 1.3 does not support ECDH
196	   [I-D.ietf-tls-rfc8446bis][RFC9147].)  This includes all cipher suites
197	   listed in the table in Appendix B.

199	   In addition, to avoid the use of non-ephemeral Diffie-Hellman,
200	   clients SHOULD NOT use and servers SHOULD NOT accept certificates
201	   with fixed DH parameters.  These certificate types are rsa_fixed_dh,
202	   dss_fixed_dh, rsa_fixed_ecdh and ecdsa_fixed_ecdh as listed in
203	   Appendix E.  These values only apply to (D)TLS versions of 1.2 and
204	   below.

206	3.  Ephemeral Finite Field Diffie-Hellman

208	   Clients MUST NOT offer and servers MUST NOT select FFDHE cipher
209	   suites in (D)TLS 1.2 connections.  This includes all cipher suites
210	   listed in the table in Appendix C.  (Note that (D)TLS 1.0 and TLS 1.1
211	   are deprecated by [RFC8996].)  FFDHE cipher suites in (D)TLS 1.3 do
212	   not suffer from the problems presented in Section 1; see
213	   [I-D.ietf-tls-rfc8446bis] and [RFC9147].  Therefore, clients and
214	   servers MAY offer FFDHE cipher suites in (D)TLS 1.3 connections.

216	4.  RSA

218	   Clients MUST NOT offer and servers MUST NOT select RSA cipher suites
219	   in (D)TLS 1.2 connections.  (Note that (D)TLS 1.0 and TLS 1.1 are
220	   deprecated by [RFC8996], and (D)TLS 1.3 does not support static RSA
221	   [I-D.ietf-tls-rfc8446bis][RFC9147].)  This includes all cipher suites
222	   listed in the table in Appendix D.  Note that these cipher suites are
223	   already marked as not recommended in the "TLS Cipher Suites"
224	   registry.

238	6.  Security Considerations

240	   Non-ephemeral finite field DH cipher suites (TLS_DH_*), as well as
241	   ephemeral key reuse for finite field DH cipher suites, are prohibited
242	   due to the [Raccoon] attack.  Both are already considered bad
243	   practice since they do not provide forward secrecy.  However, Raccoon
244	   revealed that timing side channels in processing TLS premaster
245	   secrets may be exploited to reveal the encrypted premaster secret.

247	   As for non-ephemeral elliptic curve DH cipher suites (TLS_ECDH_*),
248	   forgoing forward secrecy not only allows retroactive decryption in
249	   the event of key compromise but may also enable a broad category of
250	   attacks where the attacker exploits key reuse to repeatedly query a
251	   cryptographic secret.

253	   This category includes, but is not necessarily limited to, the
254	   following examples:

256	   1.  Invalid curve attacks, where the attacker exploits key reuse to
257	       repeatedly query and eventually learn the key itself.  These
258	       attacks have been shown to be practical against real-world TLS
259	       implementations [ICA].

261	   2.  Side channel attacks, where the attacker exploits key reuse and
262	       an additional side channel to learn a cryptographic secret.  As
263	       one example of such attacks, refer to [MAY4].

265	   3.  Fault attacks, where the attacker exploits key reuse and
266	       incorrect calculations to learn a cryptographic secret.  As one
267	       example of such attacks, see [PARIS256].

269	   Such attacks are often implementation-dependent, including the above
270	   examples.  However, these examples demonstrate that building a system
271	   that reuses keys and avoids this category of attacks is difficult in
272	   practice.  In contrast, avoiding key reuse not only prevents
273	   decryption in the event of key compromise, but also precludes this
274	   category of attacks altogether.  Therefore, this document discourages
275	   the reuse of elliptic curve DH public keys.

277	   As for ephemeral finite field Diffie-Hellman in (D)TLS 1.2 (TLS_DHE_*
278	   and TLS_PSK_DHE_*), as explained above, clients have no practical way
279	   to support these cipher suites while ensuring they only negotiate
280	   security parameters that are acceptable to them.  In (D)TLS 1.2, the
281	   server chooses the Diffie-Hellman group, and custom groups are
282	   prevalent.  Therefore, once the client includes these cipher suites
283	   in its handshake and the server presents a custom group, the client
284	   cannot complete the handshake while ensuring security.  Verifying the
285	   group structure is prohibitively expensive for the client.  Using a
286	   safelist of known-good groups is also impractical, since server
287	   operators were encouraged to generate their own custom group.
288	   Further, there is no mechanism for the handshake to fall back to
289	   other parameters, that are acceptable to both the client and server.
